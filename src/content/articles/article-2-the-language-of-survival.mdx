# The Language of Survival

## Why indirect communication is not a personality flaw but a cosmological consequence

---

Japanese has a word that English doesn't: **当てこすり** — *atekosuri*. It describes the act of saying something general, ostensibly about no one in particular, that is clearly aimed at a specific person. A manager addressing the team about punctuality when one employee has been arriving late. A mother mentioning how happy the neighbours seem with their three children while her unmarried child sits across the table. A group chat reminder to return borrowed things, posted the day after one person forgot.

The message is sent. The target receives it. Everyone in the room understands. And yet nothing was directly said.

Every culture does this. The Japanese contribution is not the behaviour but the word — which means they have identified it, named it, and implicitly recognised it as a legitimate social instrument rather than mere passive aggression.

The reason it exists follows directly from the cosmological fork established in the previous article. In a whole-first culture, meaning is not carried by the individual statement — it is carried by context. What surrounds the words matters as much as the words themselves. Indirection works because the shared context does the interpretive work. The group already knows. The atmosphere already communicates. The speaker need only gesture in the right direction.

In a part-first culture, meaning is expected to live inside the explicit statement. If you have something to say, you say it. Indirection is therefore read as evasion, weakness, or manipulation — because it violates the assumption that individuals carry their meaning directly in their words.

Neither culture, however, is actually comfortable with full directness. Direct communication has real costs everywhere: exposure, rejection, retaliation, the irreversible damage of a thing once said. Indirection is not a cultural eccentricity — it is a rational response to those costs. The culture shapes the form it takes, not the impulse itself.

Silence follows the same logic. In a whole-first culture, the relationship is assumed to exist whether or not anyone is speaking. Silence is just two people inside something that is already there. In a part-first culture, the relationship is built through interaction — which means when interaction stops, the building stops. Silence feels like the connection is dissolving, because without the conversation, there is nothing holding it together.

One culture is uncomfortable with disruption. The other is uncomfortable with silence. Both are protecting the same thing — belonging — from opposite directions.
