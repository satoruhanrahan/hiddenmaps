In ancient China there were rumours of a mystical land in the east where the immortals lived. The first emperor of China — Qin Shi Huang — wanted the secrets to living forever. So, he sent an expedition led by a man named Xu Fu to find the elixir of immortality. His fleet sailed east with thousands of men and women, seeds, and craftspeople.

## What the Historical Records Say

Chinese records state that there were two voyages led by Xu Fu. The first one was reported by Xu Fu to have reached the mystical Penglai island, but he reported that the immortals asked for a tribute of skilled archers. The second expedition, leaving in 210 BC, did not return. The location they ended up is not recorded, but there is a record stating that they found a flat and fertile land with broad plains and marshes where they settled, where Xu Fu declared himself king.

## What Actually Happened?

China at the time of these voyages was not a unified culture. The Qi region on the Shandong peninsula, where Xu Fu was from, was the heartland of a distinct spiritual tradition involving Yin Yang cosmology, occultists, alchemists, and immortality seekers. China had recently been conquered by the Qin of western China, who imposed their systems of strict law, bureaucracy, and agricultural and military productivity.

The emperor was ordering the burning of books and the execution of scholars like Xu Fu. It is not hard to believe, then, that Xu Fu may have been making up the stories of immortals asking for skilled archers and of sea creatures preventing his return. Why would an expedition for the elixir of immortality require thousands of young men and women, and seeds for agriculture? The plan from the start was probably to settle in the islands of the east, and perhaps even to defend themselves with archers.

## Was It the Founding of Japan?

In Japan there are multiple places claimed to be associated with Xu Fu. There are shrines dedicated to him, and even people who claim to be his descendants. The archaeological evidence shows that at the time of Xu Fu, Japan had for centuries been in a period of transition in which Yayoi people from the continent, coming from across the sea, brought agriculture and new technology. It is possible the Xu Fu expedition was part of a mass migration from the recently war-torn and oppressed eastern China regions towards the islands of Japan.

Japan as a nation, though, starts around the Nara period — almost a thousand years after these events. And modern Japanese culture is a mix of the native Jōmon, the incoming Yayoi, and later waves of Chinese and Korean culture. So no, Xu Fu did not found Japan — but he is part of a legend that may perhaps be the most dramatic account of the migration and influence reaching Japan from China. In Yayoi-period Japan, a large fleet of thousands of people, direct from China and backed by the imperial court, would have been quite an event for the locals.

## My Personal Speculation

Perhaps not unlike the Americas of the 1800s, Japan was a land of refuge for people seeking to escape the oppression of the old continent, in search of the freedom to hold on to their spiritual traditions. In Europe, the Romans pushed Celtic culture to the fringes, to the islands of Britain and Ireland. A similar phenomenon could have happened in East Asia. Perhaps Japan's culture of harmony, and respect for the spirits of the land, was strengthened and protected by these ancient people from China.
