Fukuzawa Yukichi has been on the ten-thousand-yen note since 1984. He is Japan's most celebrated Meiji-era intellectual — the man who introduced Western ideas of liberty, education, and independence to a modernising nation. Putting him on the highest-denomination banknote is a way of saying: this man matters.

It is also, if you read what he actually wrote, a way of safely burying him.

## A low-ranking samurai who changed Japan

Fukuzawa Yukichi (1835–1901) was born into an impoverished low-ranking samurai family in Osaka. His father died when he was young, leaving the family in difficult circumstances. He educated himself relentlessly — first in Dutch studies, then in English — and became one of the first Japanese citizens to travel to the West, joining Shogunate missions to the United States and Europe between 1860 and 1867.

What he saw there changed everything. Not just the technology — the railways, the factories, the guns — but the ideas underneath. The assumption that individuals had inherent worth regardless of birth. The expectation that citizens could challenge authority and be heard. The relationship between education and genuine freedom. He came back convinced that Japan's survival depended not just on acquiring Western technology but on absorbing Western ideas about the independence of mind.

## The books

He channelled this conviction into two works that became defining documents of the Meiji era.

*Gakumon no Susume* (An Encouragement of Learning, 1872–1876) was published as a series of pamphlets and sold in the hundreds of thousands — extraordinary for the period. Its opening passage became one of the most famous in modern Japanese history: *"It is said that heaven does not create one man above or below another man. […] And yet, looking broadly at this human world, we find wise men and ignorant men, rich men and poor men, men of high standing and men of low standing. […] Only those who study diligently and master things become people of high standing and wealth; those who remain without learning become poor and of low standing."*

> 天は人の上に人を造らず、人の下に人を造らずと言えり。（……）されども今、広くこの人間世界を見渡すに、かしこき人あり、おろかなる人あり、貧しきもあり、富めるもあり、貴人もあり、下人もありて、その有様雲と泥との相違あるに似たるは何ぞや。（……）ただ学問を勤めて物事をよく知る者は貴人となり富人となり、無学なる者は貧人となり下人となるなり。

The argument was radical for its time: hierarchy was not natural or divinely ordained. It was produced by education — or the withholding of it.

*Bunmeiron no Gairyaku* (An Outline of a Theory of Civilization, 1875) made the broader argument: that civilisation was not just material progress but the development of a particular kind of mind — independent, critical, capable of honest assessment. Japan needed to pursue both.

## The founder

He founded what became Keio University, one of Japan's most prestigious institutions. He launched the newspaper *Jiji Shimpo* in 1882, which became one of the most influential publications of the Meiji period. He was, by any measure, the most influential intellectual outside government service in nineteenth-century Japan.

His face is on the banknote. His name is on the university. His ideas about modernisation are credited with shaping modern Japan.

What is less often discussed is what he spent the later decades of his career warning about — and how completely that warning was ignored.
