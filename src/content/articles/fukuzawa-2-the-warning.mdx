The famous essay *Datsu-A Ron* (On Leaving Asia, 1885) is the piece most people associate with Fukuzawa Yukichi — the argument that Japan should separate itself from China and Korea and align with Western civilisation to survive the colonial era. This is the Fukuzawa that Japan memorialised: the pragmatic moderniser who helped the country level up fast enough to avoid being swallowed.

But running through his two major works — *Gakumon no Susume* and *Bunmeiron no Gairyaku* — is a different, deeper argument. One that Japan has been much less interested in remembering.

## National independence through personal independence

His core principle, stated repeatedly across his major works, was *dokuritsu jison* — independence and self-respect. The logic was direct: a nation cannot be genuinely free if its individual members have not developed the capacity for independent judgement. Obedience to authority, however efficient in the short term, produces a nation that depends entirely on whoever is at the top — and collapses, or goes catastrophically wrong, when that authority fails.

In *Gakumon no Susume*, he put it plainly: *"If people do not have these independent qualities of mind and are merely reliant on the power of others, the entire nation will be dependents and there will be no one to support them. It will be like a procession of blind men without a guide. Would this not be absurd?"*

> 人々この独立の心なくしてただ他人の力によりすがらんとのみせば、全国の人はみな、よりすがる人のみにてこれを引き受くる者はなかるべし。これをたとえば盲人の行列に手引きなきがごとし、はなはだ不都合ならずや。

The image is precise. A procession of blind men without a guide does not fall apart immediately. It keeps moving. It looks organised. The crisis comes when the path turns, or drops away — and there is nobody in the procession capable of seeing it.

## The surface without the substance

In *Bunmeiron no Gairyaku*, he made the distinction between the external forms of civilisation and its animating spirit explicit. Japan needed not just the technologies and institutions of the West but the underlying culture of intellectual independence that had produced them. Without that, modernisation would be mimicry — impressive on the surface, hollow underneath.

He was issuing this warning in 1875, less than a decade into the Meiji period. The railways were being built. The universities were opening. The constitution was being drafted. From the outside, it looked like transformation.

Fukuzawa was asking a different question: were the people inside those institutions developing the capacity to think independently — or were they simply learning to obey a new set of authorities with more modern titles?

## Why this was uncomfortable

The Meiji project needed compliance. Mobilising an entire nation at extraordinary speed to avoid colonisation required discipline, coordination, and the subordination of individual preference to collective purpose. Fukuzawa understood this — he was not naive about the pressures Japan faced.

But he insisted that the emergency logic had to be temporary. A nation that trained its people in obedience as a permanent condition was not building strength. It was building a sophisticated dependency — one that would eventually produce exactly the kind of collective failure that no amount of external modernisation could prevent.

The question was whether Japan would internalise that distinction. The answer, increasingly, was no.
