To understand what Fukuzawa Yukichi was pushing against, and why the distinction matters, you need to understand how disagreement actually worked in Tokugawa Japan — because it is more complicated than it first appears.

Tokugawa feudal society was not without mechanisms for truth-telling. It had a tradition of remonstrance — *kangen* — in which a loyal retainer was not just permitted but arguably obligated to tell their lord when a decision was wrong. This was a genuine and recognised social function. Retainers who failed to remonstrate when they saw serious mistakes were sometimes criticised for their silence.

## What remonstrance actually was

But the conditions governing remonstrance were very strict. It had to be done privately, not publicly. It had to be framed as loyal concern rather than criticism — the retainer was not challenging the lord's authority but offering information the lord might lack. It had to follow proper channels within the hierarchy. And crucially, the retainer accepted in advance that the lord could reject the remonstrance entirely and the retainer would submit — or in extreme cases, die for the principle without expecting vindication.

The key point: remonstrance was permitted *within* prescribed hierarchical relationships, and its purpose was to make the hierarchy function better. It was not a mechanism for questioning whether the hierarchy itself was right.

What was not permitted was anything outside those prescribed relationships. Public criticism was dangerous. Horizontal criticism — challenging peers, not superiors — violated norms. Criticism of the shogunate itself, or of the social order as a whole, was suppressed. And the idea that an ordinary individual might stand outside the entire system and evaluate it against independent standards — that was not just dangerous but genuinely culturally unintelligible.

## The critical distinction

This is what Fukuzawa was actually pushing for, and what Tokugawa Japan genuinely lacked. Not better internal feedback loops within the hierarchy — those existed, imperfectly. But people capable of what he called genuine independence of mind: the ability to step outside the whole system and ask whether it was right, regardless of what the hierarchy said.

The remonstrance tradition was a pressure valve. Properly used, it could correct individual bad decisions within an otherwise functioning hierarchy. What it could not do — what it was specifically not designed to do — was correct the hierarchy itself when the hierarchy as a whole was going wrong.

Fukuzawa had read enough Western history to know what that distinction meant in practice. A political system that can correct individual decisions but not systemic errors is stable until it isn't — and when it fails, it fails completely, because all the internal correction mechanisms are designed to reinforce the system rather than question it.

## What he wanted instead

In *Gakumon no Susume*, he wrote: *"In France there were many patriotic citizens, each of whom took the responsibility of the nation's crisis upon himself and fought for his own land without needing to be persuaded by others. From this we can infer how, in a crisis calling for national defense, those who have the spirit of independence will exhibit deep concern for their country, in contrast to those who have not."*

> フランスには報国の士民多くして国の難を銘々の身に引き受け、人の勧めを待たずしてみずから本国のために戦う者あるゆえ、かかる相違もできしことなり。これによりて考うれば、外国へ対して自国を守るに当たり、その国人に独立の気力ある者は国を思うこと深切にして、独立の気力なき者は不深切なること推して知るべきなり。

The contrast he was drawing was between people who act from internal conviction and people who act because the hierarchy tells them to. Both might produce the same behaviour in ordinary times. In a crisis — when the hierarchy itself is failing — only the first type can respond usefully. The second type keeps following instructions until there are no more instructions to follow.

This was what he wanted to build. It was also precisely what the Meiji project, as it developed through the 1880s and 1890s, was in the process of systematically dismantling.
