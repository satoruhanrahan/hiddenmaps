Fukuzawa Yukichi lived until 1901. He had thirty-three years of Meiji Japan to observe — long enough to watch the project he had helped shape be captured by a logic he had specifically warned against.

The early Meiji years had genuine energy. New ideas poured in. The sense that Japan was remaking itself from the ground up was real, and Fukuzawa's *Gakumon no Susume* was a bestseller not because people were forced to read it but because the argument landed. The idea that individual independence was both possible and necessary felt like a genuine break from the past.

By the mid-1880s, the mood had shifted.

## The architecture of compliance

The Meiji Constitution of 1889 concentrated imperial authority in ways that left little institutional room for the culture of independent political judgement Fukuzawa had advocated. The Imperial Rescript on Education of 1890 made loyalty and filial piety — rather than independent thought — the explicit moral foundation of the school system. The language of civilisation was being steadily colonised by the language of national discipline.

What the Meiji state was building — particularly through State Shinto and the Imperial Rescript — was a mechanism for elevating criticism of the national project to something approaching sacrilege. In the Tokugawa period, questioning authority had been dangerous but comprehensible — a political act within a political system. The Meiji state was working to make questioning the national narrative feel cosmologically disordered: not just politically risky but morally wrong at a fundamental level.

This was precisely what Fukuzawa had warned about. He had argued in *Bunmeiron no Gairyaku* that the civilisation of a nation cannot advance solely through the power of government — that it required the cultivation of independent moral agency in ordinary people. What Japan was building instead was a more sophisticated version of Tokugawa compliance, dressed in constitutional and parliamentary clothing.

## Being heard — and not

He wrote about this. Through *Jiji Shimpo*, the newspaper he founded in 1882, he was one of the most prominent voices in Meiji public life. He was not a marginal figure being ignored from the outside — he was the most influential intellectual outside government service of the entire period. His misgivings about the growing dependence of citizens on the state are documented in his later works, *Fukuō Hyakuwa* (One Hundred Discourses of Fukuzawa, 1897) and his *Autobiography* (1899).

And he was, in the specific warning that mattered most, not listened to.

His philosophy of *dokuritsu jison* — independence and self-respect, the principle that individuals should think and act for themselves even when it goes against the grain — never became the animating principle of the Meiji educational or political project. The principle was celebrated. The implication — that citizens should be genuinely willing to challenge state authority when it was wrong — was quietly set aside.

## What he did not live to see

He died in 1901, before the worst of what followed. He did not live to see the militarist period, the Pacific War, or the specific catastrophes that his analysis would have predicted. But the architecture was already in place by the time he died: a nation with modern institutions and a fundamentally unchanged relationship between the individual and authority.

The external forms of civilisation had accumulated impressively. The internal transformation — the development of genuinely independent minds — had not happened. The surface without the substance: exactly what he had warned about in 1875.
