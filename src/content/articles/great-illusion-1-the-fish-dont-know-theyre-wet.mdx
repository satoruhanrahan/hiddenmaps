Try something. Think about how you'd invite a friend to join you for dinner. The natural form in English is almost automatic: *do you want to come?*

Now consider what that question assumes.

It assumes your friend has a clear personal preference that's worth locating. It assumes that preference should be the deciding factor. It assumes that handing someone a choice is inherently an act of consideration — that centering the individual is what respect looks like.

None of this is stated. It doesn't need to be. It feels like basic courtesy.

In much of East Asia, that same question can land as faintly strange. Not because people don't have preferences, but because the question imposes a framework they don't naturally operate inside. It cuts the person loose from the shared context that would normally guide the interaction. It makes them responsible for the outcome. And it refuses to read the situation on their behalf — which in a more collectively oriented world is actually part of what considerate people do. A good host doesn't ask what you want. They read you, and provide it.

The question that feels like politeness from one side feels like a small abdication from the other.

Most people who grow up in Western countries carry a complete set of assumptions about how human beings work. Not opinions. Assumptions. The kind that sit below the level of thought, that have never created friction because everyone around them shares them. What a self is. What respect looks like. What it means to offer someone a choice.

These assumptions feel like rationality itself. Like what any reasonable person, anywhere, would conclude if they simply thought clearly.

That feeling is the illusion.

The differences between Western and non-Western social worlds are not surface differences — different food, different customs, different aesthetics. The operating systems are different. The model of what a person fundamentally is is different. A person fluent in one system will regularly misread situations governed by the other, walk away from interactions that failed without knowing they failed, understand things they did not actually understand.

The dinner invitation is one small example. The operating system differences run deeper than any single moment can show — into how conflict works, how truth works, how obligation works, how the self is structured.

The fish does not know it is wet. It has never had reason to notice the water.

You have assumptions you have never had cause to question. That's where we're starting.
