Picture a meeting. Ten people around a table. A junior employee has noticed something important — a flaw in the plan being discussed, something worth raising. In a Western workplace the expectation is clear: speak up. That's what good employees do. That's what the company needs.

In a Japanese workplace the same employee will often stay silent. Not because they haven't noticed. Not because they're afraid. Because their position doesn't call for them to speak yet. The hierarchy assigns who leads, who follows, who speaks first and who waits. The junior employee sitting quietly isn't being suppressed. They're doing their job correctly.

The Western observer watching this scene feels something. Probably discomfort. Possibly moral concern. Someone with something valuable to say isn't saying it, and a system is responsible. That feeling is worth examining — because it isn't a neutral response. It's an assumption revealing itself.

The assumption is that individual expression is inherently valuable. That not speaking is inherently a loss. That a system which assigns roles is inherently doing something to people that they would resist if they could.

But the junior employee isn't experiencing suppression. They're experiencing clarity. In a flat Western hierarchy — and it is a hierarchy, just an unnamed one — every meeting contains an invisible contest. Who speaks first. Who takes up most space. Who gets listened to and who gets talked over. Nobody assigns these positions. They're fought for, subtly, every time. The person who wins is usually the most confident, the most verbally fluent, the most comfortable performing authority.

In Japan the position is simply known. The junior employee doesn't have to fight for their place or worry about overstepping or calculate whether this is their moment. The structure holds them. There is a particular kind of freedom in that — one that's almost impossible to feel from outside the system.

This is not an argument that Japanese hierarchy is without cost. It has real ones. It is an argument that the Western reader's instinct — that hierarchy is something done to people against their nature — is not a universal human intuition. It's a specific cultural assumption dressed as common sense.

Western culture has hierarchy. It just refuses to name it. Wealth, confidence, connections — these create real structures of access and authority that operate freely precisely because nobody acknowledges them. At least Japanese hierarchy comes with obligations. The senior person owes something to those below. The unnamed Western version makes no such demands.

The fish doesn't know it's wet. But the water has a shape.
