Most people who travel return feeling like they've encountered something real. They ate food that wasn't made for tourists. They got lost. They had conversations with locals. They came back changed, or at least expanded. They feel, with some justification, that they made a genuine effort.

But consider what they actually moved through.

The economy built around visitors selects for people comfortable operating in foreign-facing mode. The hotels, the restaurants, the tourist sites, the English-speaking guides — these are not random samples of the culture. They are interfaces. Points where the local system has been adapted, consciously or not, to produce interactions that work for outsiders. The traveler moves through two weeks of these interfaces and experiences them as the place itself.

This isn't deception. Nobody is performing falsely. It is simply what happens when two operating systems meet and one side has learned to do the translation work.

In Japan this goes deeper than tourist infrastructure. Japanese social behaviour is fundamentally high-context — meaning the situation determines the response. Who you're talking to, what their position is, what the context requires — these factors don't just affect your tone. They determine which social mode you operate in. A conversation with a senior colleague, a close friend, a stranger on the street, a foreign visitor — these aren't variations on the same interaction. They are different interactions, requiring genuinely different selves.

The foreign visitor is a context. It calls for a particular response. Not always the same response — it depends on the foreigner, the situation, the implicit status signals being read. But it is always a response to what the situation requires, not simply to who the person is.

The Western visitor has no idea this is happening. Not because they're unsophisticated, but because their operating system doesn't work this way. In a low-context culture you are largely the same person across situations. More formal here, more relaxed there — but not fundamentally switching modes. The concept that your conversation partner has read the context and selected an appropriate social self doesn't exist as a live possibility. So it doesn't occur to them.

The Japanese person, meanwhile, cannot quite imagine someone being unaware that this switching happens. In their world everyone does it. It's not conscious — it's just correct social behaviour. The idea that a person could move through social situations without reading the context and adjusting accordingly is genuinely difficult to conceive.

So the Westerner's unawareness reads as something else entirely.

Someone who doesn't adjust to the context — who walks in and acts as they would anywhere, as if the situation makes no special demands — is, from inside a hierarchical high-context culture, someone who doesn't feel the need to adjust. And someone who doesn't feel the need to adjust is someone who considers themselves above the social negotiation.

The Westerner accidentally performs dominance through sheer unawareness. They make an implicit status claim they don't know they're making. The Japanese person reads that claim and responds to it — accommodating, adjusting, switching into whatever mode the situation now calls for. Which the Westerner experiences as the interaction going fine.

Both people did exactly what their operating system told them to do. Neither noticed what the other was actually doing. And the gap between the systems, which is large enough that you'd expect it to be undeniable, stayed completely invisible.

Every culture has some version of the managed foreign encounter. But the sophistication of the interface depends on what's already built into the social operating system.

In a low-context culture the self is largely consistent across situations. You adjust your tone, your formality, your register — but you don't fundamentally switch modes. So when a Westerner encounters a foreigner, what they present is more or less what they present to everyone. The interface is thin because the underlying technology for switching doesn't really exist.

In high-context cultures the switching is already built into daily social life. Every encounter requires reading the context and selecting the appropriate mode. The foreign encounter is just another context — one that calls for its own particular response. The interface is more complete and more invisible precisely because the social equipment for producing it is already highly developed.

The Westerner's thin interface is itself an example of the operating system in action — the same self, exported everywhere, without knowing that's what's happening.

The traveler comes home feeling changed. They were changed. They just encountered a smaller gap than the one that actually exists — one built, without anyone deciding to build it, to be crossable.

The larger gap stayed where it was.
