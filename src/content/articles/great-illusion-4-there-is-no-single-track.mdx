You probably believe, somewhere in the back of your mind, that given enough time and development most countries will end up resembling liberal democracies. Maybe not identical. But roughly heading there. Free press, individual rights, open elections, rule of law. It feels like realism. It might even feel like optimism — faith that people everywhere want the same things and will eventually get them.

It's worth looking at that belief more carefully.

Get where, exactly? And who decided that was the destination?

The desire to not be oppressed is universal. Nobody anywhere enjoys being arbitrarily imprisoned or having their life controlled by someone with unchecked power. That much is shared. But the specific institutional form that protection takes in Western liberal democracy — individual rights held against the state, elected representatives, freedom of expression as a near-absolute value — that's not universal. It's a specific solution that developed in a specific place over a specific history.

Japanese people don't fear hierarchical authority the way Westerners do. Not because they haven't figured it out yet. Because their operating system distributes security and identity differently. The hierarchy holds people as much as it constrains them. The absence of Western-style individualism doesn't register as a lack from inside that system. It registers as a different set of tradeoffs — ones that make sense given everything else the system does.

The same goes for science, critical thinking, individual rights. These feel universal because they're built into the infrastructure of modern global life. You can't fully participate in the modern world without them. But that's evidence of dominance, not universality. These things got everywhere because one particular civilisation got powerful enough to build the roads, the institutions, the universities, the economic systems. The framework travelled with the infrastructure.

A river that gets wide enough starts to look like the landscape itself.

That's the illusion at its deepest. Not that you think your culture is better — you probably don't. It's that in these areas you don't experience it as culture at all. Democracy doesn't feel like a Western value. It feels like what free people eventually choose. Science doesn't feel like a Western method. It feels like how you find out what's true. The framework disappears into the background and becomes the shape of reality itself.

So when you look at a country that hasn't adopted Western liberal institutions, in your mind, without you deciding this, it becomes a society that hasn't arrived yet.

Except it isn't behind. It's elsewhere.
