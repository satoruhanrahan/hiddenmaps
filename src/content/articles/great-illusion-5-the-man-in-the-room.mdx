In the winter of 1619 a French philosopher sat alone in a heated room and decided to doubt everything. René Descartes wanted to find something solid — something that couldn't be taken away by culture, tradition, or inherited belief. He would strip it all back until he hit bedrock. What remained after everything uncertain was removed would be pure reason itself. Universal. Uncontaminated. Human.

It was a genuinely ambitious idea. It was also the most culturally specific move imaginable.

The decision to strip away tradition to find truth assumes that tradition is the problem. That removing context gets you closer to reality. That the individual mind, reasoning alone, is the right instrument for finding what's real. These aren't neutral assumptions. They're the product of a specific European intellectual history that had spent centuries fighting against the authority of inherited religion and political power. Descartes wasn't escaping culture. He was expressing it at its most concentrated.

He thought he'd found the universal structure of human thought. And the method he developed was genuinely powerful. Science, mathematics, the foundations of Western philosophy — these built on what that room produced. The outputs were impressive enough that the framework hitched a ride on them. If the method generates results, surely the method is correct. Surely this is just how reason works.

But then you look at what other traditions built without that room.

Buddhist epistemology doesn't strip away context to find truth — it treats context as inseparable from understanding. Confucian ethics doesn't start with the individual and build outward — it starts with relationships as the fundamental unit of reality. These aren't failed attempts at Cartesian thinking. They're different starting points that lead somewhere else entirely. And they work. They produced sophisticated philosophy, ethics, art, and social organisation across centuries and billions of lives.

If Descartes had found the universal structure of human thought you'd expect other traditions to have converged on something similar independently. Instead what you find is genuine divergence. The diversity of sophisticated human thought systems is the quiet empirical disproof of the universality claim.

Descartes found something real. He found one powerful way of approaching certain kinds of problems. What he didn't find — couldn't have found, from inside the only tradition he had access to — was the view from nowhere. There is no view from nowhere. There is only ever a view from somewhere.

He thought he'd left the water. He was swimming in it the whole time.
