Occasionally someone breaks through.

A Westerner who has spent years in Japan, not moving through the tourist layer but actually embedded — learning the language, building real relationships, slowly accumulating the kind of knowledge that doesn't come from books. They start to see behind the interface. They respond to things that weren't meant to be visible. They read a situation correctly that they were never supposed to read.

And something interesting happens. The Japanese person they're talking to becomes slightly uncomfortable.

Not because anything went wrong. Because something went right that wasn't supposed to. The performance — the natural, unconscious accommodation that happens when a Westerner is present — stopped working as expected. Someone reached behind it.

This reveals something important. The interface isn't just practical accommodation. It's built on a prior assumption — that genuine understanding across this particular gap isn't really possible. That the Westerner simply doesn't have the equipment. So the pragmatic move isn't to show them the real operating system. It's to produce a version of the interaction they can handle.

But here's where it becomes a loop.

The assumption causes the performance. The performance prevents genuine understanding. The prevention confirms the assumption. Nobody decided any of this. It runs automatically, on both sides, every time the two operating systems meet.

The Westerner leaves having experienced a managed interaction they experienced as real. The Japanese person leaves confirmed in their belief that genuine understanding wasn't possible — because it didn't happen. Both are right about what occurred. Neither sees why it occurred.

This isn't true of every Japanese person. Someone who has spent time abroad, worked closely with foreigners, or had genuine cross-cultural friendships has already had the loop broken. They've met the exception and updated accordingly. Their assumption shifts — not to 'all Westerners understand' but to 'some might, and it's worth finding out.' The performance drops earlier, genuine contact becomes more likely, the assumption softens further.

The loop is most total for people whose entire experience of foreigners has confirmed it. Which, for most Japanese people who've spent their lives in Japan, is most of them. The assumption isn't irrational. It's just built on a skewed sample.

The loop can break from either side. But it's not equally easy. The Japanese person at least knows it exists. The Westerner doesn't even know there's a behind to get behind.
