You haven't heard any of this before. Not in school, not in the media, not in casual conversation. And it isn't because the knowledge doesn't exist. Researchers have documented it. People who've lived across cultures know it intimately. The evidence is there.

The problem is that saying it clearly comes with a cost.

The moment you make a strong claim about how a particular group of people think — even a careful, evidence-based, non-hierarchical claim — you risk something. Not just criticism. Something earlier than that. The association with racism activates before the argument is even finished. People's faces change. The conversation shifts from what you're saying to why you might be saying it.

Most people learn this quickly and adjust. They soften the claim. They add so many qualifications it loses its shape. Or they stay quiet. Not because they were wrong but because being right isn't worth what saying it costs.

This is more effective than censorship. Censorship creates martyrs. It drives knowledge underground where it survives and spreads. What happened here is different — people learned to stop themselves before they even got to the point of speaking. The self-censorship happens at the level of thought formation, not expression. The result is people who genuinely believe they're thinking freely while avoiding entire categories of perception.

The illusion doesn't need to be actively maintained. It maintains itself.

The caution has real reasons behind it. The history of talking about cultural difference is genuinely contaminated — by racial hierarchy, by colonial condescension, by people who used the language of difference to justify domination. That history is close enough that the wariness makes sense. The problem is when caution hardens into reflex. When the reasonable instinct to avoid prejudice becomes an inability to perceive difference at all. That's not open-mindedness. It's a different kind of blindness.

And so the fish stays wet. Not because no one noticed the water. Because every time someone pointed at it, the pointing finger got more attention than the water itself.

The series started with a simple question — what do you assume without knowing you assume it? The answer turned out to be almost everything that feels most natural and most obvious about how people work. That's not a comfortable conclusion. But discomfort is usually where something real is.

You're in the water. Now you know it's there.
