*It is a question most people have noticed but few discuss directly.*

Walk through any major Australian city and interracial couples are common — but one pairing appears far less often than others. Asian women with non-Asian partners is unremarkable. Asian men with non-Asian partners is noticeably rare.

---

## The data confirms it

This is not just casual observation. Dating app data confirms it consistently. Across platforms and countries, Asian men receive the lowest match and response rates from non-Asian women of any demographic group. In Australia, a country that prides itself on multiculturalism, the pattern is as pronounced as anywhere.

The common explanation is that this is simply a matter of personal preference — that attraction is instinctive, individual, and no one can be blamed for who they do and don't find appealing. There is some truth in that. But it raises an obvious question: why do so many individual preferences point in the same direction?

> Preferences that cluster this consistently across an entire population are not purely personal. They are cultural. And cultural preferences have histories.

---

## What this series argues

This series traces that history. It is not a simple story and it does not have a single villain. What it has is a sequence of distinct historical forces — each operating in its own time and place, each reinforcing what came before — that together produced the pattern visible in Australian cities and dating apps today.

Those forces include the deliberate reengineering of Western masculinity in Victorian England, the emasculation of Chinese workers on the Australian goldfields, a century of Hollywood casting decisions, the evolutionary and agricultural histories that produced genuinely different masculine ideals in East and West, and the aesthetic vocabulary that Japanese culture developed independently — one that the English language has never had adequate words for.

Understanding where a preference comes from does not make it disappear. But it does make it harder to experience as simply natural — as just the way things are.

That is what this series is for. Not to tell anyone what to find attractive, but to show that what feels instinctive has a history. And that history is more interesting, and more revealing, than most people expect.
