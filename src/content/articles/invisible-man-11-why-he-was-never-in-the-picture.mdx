*This series began with a measurable fact — Asian men receive disproportionately low interest from non-Asian women in Western countries, including Australia. It ends with an explanation.*

Not a simple one. What looks like instinctive preference turns out to be the accumulated residue of several distinct historical forces, each reinforcing the last.

---

## The through-line

Victorian England manufactured a masculine template and exported it globally through imperial reach. The Australian gold rush institutionalised the emasculation of Chinese men at the exact moment that template was arriving. Hollywood spent a century ensuring Asian men never occupied the romantic roles that build cultural desirability. And through all of it an entirely different and coherent masculine aesthetic was developing in East Asia — one that the Western framework simply lacked the vocabulary to see.

The most revealing thread running through all of this is that the masculine template was never just about attraction. It was always also about power. Victorian Britain needed hard men for imperial administration. Gold rush Australia needed a way to justify excluding effective Chinese labour. Hollywood needed heroes who looked a particular way. Xi's China needs men who project national strength. In every case the definition of masculinity was shaped by what those in power found useful.

> The men inside that template paid a price too — a culture that makes it genuinely difficult to open up, show vulnerability, or ask for help without fear of seeming weak or lesser.

---

## The diversity underneath

But underneath all of that cultural shaping, human beings are naturally diverse — in both Western and Asian populations. Tall and short, angular and soft-featured, loud and quiet, dominant and gentle. That diversity exists everywhere. And desire is most likely just as diverse underneath — a much wider range of genuine human attraction than any single culture's standards suggest.

What culture does is narrow that range without people realising it. It decides which qualities are visible and which are not. It shapes what feels instinctive until the construction becomes invisible — until a preference that was made feels like one that was always there.

---

## What changes

Attractiveness standards have changed dramatically throughout history and will change again. The template that feels permanent today is no more fixed than the Romantic ideal that preceded it — or the bishounen aesthetic that flourished in post-war Japan when the previous template was discredited.

Most people never question what they find attractive. That is precisely how culture does its work.
