*In 1851 gold was discovered in Victoria. Within months tens of thousands of men from around the world had descended on regional Australia with one intention — to get rich as fast as possible.*

---

## A different way of working

Among them were thousands of Chinese men, mostly from Guangdong province, who came with something the European diggers largely did not bring: a cooperative working culture refined over centuries of collective agriculture back home.

Where European diggers typically worked their claims alone or in small informal groups, Chinese workers organised systematically. They pooled resources, divided labour efficiently, worked exhausted claims that individuals had abandoned, and extracted gold that others had missed. By almost any measure they were more effective.

The reaction from European diggers was not admiration. It was fury.

---

## Strength reframed as weakness

But the fury was framed in a revealing way. The Chinese were not accused simply of being better organised. They were accused of being unfair — of cheating — of winning through methods that a real man would not use. The implicit argument was that legitimate gold mining meant individual effort and individual reward. Working collectively was somehow not playing by the rules.

This framing did something important. It took a genuine strength — cooperative efficiency — and recast it as a moral deficiency. The Chinese worker was not seen as disciplined and organised. He was seen as clannish, unable to stand alone, dependent on the group.

> The same behaviour read completely differently through the lens of the Victorian masculine template that had just been exported from Britain.

---

## From goldfields to legislation

The economic anxiety quickly became cultural and then legislative. Chinese immigration was restricted in several colonies through the 1850s. By 1901 the Immigration Restriction Act — the foundation of the White Australia Policy — was among the first legislation passed by the new federal parliament.

The stereotype that calcified during this period — the Asian man as industrious but unmasculine, effective but not admirable — did not disappear when the policy eventually did.

It simply stopped needing a law to hold it in place.
