*When Bruce Lee became an international star in the early 1970s he was arguably the most physically compelling action hero on screen. Audiences worldwide watched him move with a speed and precision no Western actor could match. He was magnetic, confident, and utterly watchable.*

He almost never kissed anyone on screen.

---

## The unwritten rule

This was not an accident. Hollywood had an unwritten rule, rarely stated explicitly but consistently applied — Asian men did not get the girl. They could fight, they could be wise, they could provide comic relief, but the romantic plot was not available to them. The hero got the love interest. The Asian man got the next scene.

The pattern persisted long after Lee. Jackie Chan spent decades as one of Hollywood's biggest stars, appearing in film after film without a romantic storyline. In *Rush Hour*, one of the highest grossing action comedies of the 1990s, his character exists almost entirely outside the romantic world the film otherwise takes for granted.

---

## The most damaging example

The most damaging example is arguably Long Duk Dong in the 1984 film *Sixteen Candles* — a character so broad and humiliating in his portrayal that his name became shorthand for the desexualised, socially incompetent Asian male stereotype. An entire generation of American teenagers absorbed that image at an impressionable age.

> Films and television do not simply reflect existing cultural attitudes — they produce and normalise them.

A generation that grows up never seeing Asian men in romantic lead roles does not consciously decide Asian men are unattractive. They simply absorb that absence as the natural order of things.

---

## The opposite treatment

The contrast with how Asian women were treated makes the pattern starker. Asian women were frequently exoticised in Western film — hypersexualised rather than desexualised, mysterious and available rather than invisible. The distortion ran in opposite directions, but both served the same underlying purpose — positioning Asian people as supporting characters in a story whose heroes were always Western.

The camera has enormous power to shape desire. For most of the 20th century it consistently looked away.
