*In 2021 China's national broadcasting authority issued new guidelines for television and online media. Male celebrities were not to appear overly feminine. Soft features, careful styling, and emotional expressiveness were flagged as problematic.*

The term the state used was *niang pao* — roughly translating as girly or effeminate. The message was direct: this aesthetic had no place in the national media landscape.

---

## A familiar justification

The justification was framed in terms of national strength. Soft-looking men, the argument went, produced a soft society — one insufficiently prepared for geopolitical competition and military readiness. China needed men who looked and behaved like men, by a definition the state was now making explicit.

This logic will sound familiar to anyone who has read the previous articles in this series.

Victorian Britain made an almost identical argument in the mid-19th century. The empire needed a particular kind of man — physically tough, emotionally controlled, unambiguously dominant. Institutions were deliberately reformed to produce him. Sensitivity was not merely unfashionable. It was treated as a threat to national capability.

> Two very different governments, separated by 150 years and vast cultural distance, reached for exactly the same tool when they felt their power required a particular kind of male citizen.

---

## What both were reacting to

In Victorian Britain it was the Romantic poet. In Xi's China it is the K-pop influenced idol — smooth-skinned, carefully styled, emotionally expressive, and enormously popular with young audiences the state would prefer to reach with different messages.

K-pop masculinity is particularly threatening to this kind of control because it built its appeal entirely outside state systems. No government produced it. No institution engineered it. It spread through genuine audience preference across cultural boundaries — including into Australia and the broader West.

---

## What this reveals

That the Chinese state and Western cultural assumptions are both uncomfortable with the same aesthetic, for different reasons, says something worth noticing.

The masculine template has never been just about what men look like. It has always been about what those in power need men to be. When that need changes, the template changes with it. And when an aesthetic emerges that no one engineered and no one controls, the institutions that depend on that template tend to react.
