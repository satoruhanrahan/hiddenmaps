# Is Christianity True? The Philosophical Case

*The evidence is inconclusive, but perhaps that is the point.*

Looking at the cases for and against Christianity, we find it is impossible to come to one final conclusion. We hit an inconclusive dead end. It could be true or it could not be true. This is frustrating, but just like many other puzzles in life, maybe the best way to solve the problem is to change our frame of mind.

## The free will argument

In the Bible it says God wants a relationship with us. If you were the God of the Bible, and you wanted to form a good relationship with the humans of the world, what would you do? A major part of a good relationship is trust. You communicate to one another, you care for one another, and you trust one another. An ideal relationship is not forced; it is something that both parties choose to be a part of.

The thing is though, a relationship with another human is different to a relationship with God. One is with an equal, and the other is with an all-knowing and all-powerful creator of the world. This creates a dynamic where the proof of the existence of God would force us to follow God. With this in mind, perhaps the best way for God to form relationships with humans is to create a world in which there is some evidence, but not proof. This would allow us the freedom to choose whether to believe in God or not to believe in God.

A world that allows people to get to know God themselves and to choose to trust in God would therefore be a world with some evidence, but not too much evidence. There would still be some room for doubt. Trust only exists in a situation in which there is some doubt. By removing all doubt, you remove the need to trust. And without the need for trust, you are in a forced situation. Not a true loving relationship.

If we look through the evidence as we did in the previous article, this is exactly what we find. We have the freedom to believe God does not exist, but we also have good reason to trust what God may be communicating to us through Jesus, his disciples, and the Bible. And this freedom may be the perfect way for us to form the kind of relationship that God is envisioning for us.

## The three choices

We are left with three choices after considering all of this, and each choice makes sense intellectually. You can trust the testimony of the people who gave up their lives to say they saw Jesus alive again. You can conclude that they were sincere but mistaken — that grief somehow made them believe they saw Jesus alive again. Or you can hold the question open and choose not to trust but to wait for further evidence.

What does not make much sense though is to say confidently that Christianity is true or not true. The real question becomes whether this uncertainty is the whole point. Whether the uncertainty is deliberately being calibrated for us, or whether it is just a random series of strange events.
