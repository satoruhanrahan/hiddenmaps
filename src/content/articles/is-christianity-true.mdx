This is one of the most loaded questions in human history, and it usually gets answered in one of two unsatisfying ways: fervent yes with no engagement with the difficulties, or dismissive no with no engagement with the evidence. Here's an attempt at something more honest.

## What we can say with reasonable confidence

Most historians — including secular ones with no stake in the theological outcome — accept that Jesus of Nazareth was a real historical figure who was executed by crucifixion under Pontius Pilate, probably around 30 CE. The evidence comes not just from the New Testament but from Roman and Jewish sources including Tacitus and Josephus, who had no interest in promoting Christianity.

We can also say with reasonable confidence that within a very short time of Jesus's death, a group of his followers began claiming he had risen from the dead — and that this belief spread rapidly, at significant personal cost to the people spreading it. People don't typically die for things they know to be fabricated.

## The strongest case for

The most compelling argument for taking Christianity seriously isn't the miracles. It's the transformation. The group of frightened, scattered disciples who hid after the crucifixion became, within weeks, a movement willing to die for their claims. Something happened. The explanation that most simply accounts for everything that followed is that they believed, with absolute conviction, that they had seen the risen Jesus.

The New Testament manuscript evidence is also stronger than most people realise — over 5,800 Greek manuscripts, many written within decades of the events, making it far better attested than almost any other ancient document.

## The strongest case against

Extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence, and the evidence for the resurrection is ancient, filtered through communities with strong reasons to present it favourably, and impossible to independently verify. The history of religion also shows that devoted communities regularly generate sincere belief in events that did not happen. The disciples may have genuinely believed they saw Jesus — and still have been wrong.

## Three verdicts on the disciples

Ultimately the historical question reduces to a judgement about the people who started the movement. The disciples either genuinely saw what they claimed — or they were mistaken, grief and unusual experiences producing sincere but false conviction. Or they were lying: a deliberate fabrication, maintained under torture and execution by men who knew it wasn't true.

Most historians, including sceptical ones, find the third option very hard to sustain. People do not die in agony for claims they know to be false. That leaves the first two: genuine experience, or genuine but mistaken experience.

Which of those you find more plausible comes down to something prior to the evidence — how much you trust the testimony of people you cannot cross-examine, and how you weigh the extraordinary against the merely very unusual. That is not purely a historical question. It is a question about how you read people.
