A very loaded topic and question. Some will shout "yes" without any doubt. Others will laugh and say, "of course not". What this article will do is grapple with what evidence there actually is, honestly and rationally.

## What we can all agree on

The consensus among historians is that Jesus existed, and that he was executed by crucifixion under Pontius Pilate around 30 AD. Besides the New Testament, there is evidence for this in Roman and Jewish sources, including writings by Tacitus and Josephus, who were not Christian themselves.

What the evidence also says is that shortly after the death of Jesus, a group of his followers began claiming that Jesus had risen from the dead. This belief spread rapidly, despite the threat of punishment and death for spreading it. People do not usually risk their lives to say things that they know is not true, so it is safe to say that they believed what they were saying.

## The case for

The most compelling reason to believe in Christianity is the transformation of the disciples. After the crucifixion of Jesus, his disciples were frightened and in hiding. It seemed like the end of it. But shortly after, they had started a movement, making incredible claims which they were willing to die for. Groups of people do not normally change in this way. Something unusual definitely happened. And why couldn't they find Jesus' body? It obviously wasn't in the tomb, or anywhere else people would look, as the authorities said that someone must have stolen the body.

The disciples believed with absolute conviction that they had seen the risen Jesus, and the people they told this to also believed them. The main source for this is in the New Testament, which besides what many may think, is 99% the same as when it was written — with Paul's letters being written around 20 years after the crucifixion. Historians say that the New Testament is one of the most reliable documents of the time period. We have found over 5000 Greek copies of the New Testament, compared to fewer than a dozen for other classical works such as Caesar's Gallic Wars. The reason they do not call it history is that the miracles it describes are hard to believe.

## The case against

When you go through the most compelling cases against the claims that Jesus rose from the dead, they boil down to the fact that this does not usually happen. People do not rise from the dead. People usually cannot turn water to wine or heal the sick instantly. And so, the most likely explanation must be that this is some sort of psychological phenomenon. Just because they believe Jesus rose from the dead, does not mean it actually happened.

There are many cases around the world throughout history of people believing in things we consider to not be true. There are many reasons and incentives for people to be convinced of things that have not happened, and this could simply be another case. This case is genuinely hard to explain, but that does not mean that a miracle had occurred. There are other more mundane possibilities, however rare and unusual.

## The two main possibilities

It comes down to what you think of the disciples who started the movement. They either genuinely saw Jesus alive again, or they were mistaken. It is possible they were lying, but why would they risk their lives for it? Lying does not make much sense. Another possible explanation sceptics give is that maybe they looked in the wrong tomb, but surely, they would have noticed and searched the right tomb. What is easier to imagine is the grief they were experiencing resulting in something unusual psychologically, producing a sincere conviction within the group of people that they had seen Jesus alive again when in fact they had not.

Usually though, it is not possible for multiple people to have the same hallucination at the same time. And the rapid spread of the belief despite persecution even among many Jewish people, upending centuries of unwavering tradition, is genuinely astonishing. You cannot prove it and you cannot disprove it. They seem equally likely to me, or should I say equally unlikely. We are confronted with two explanations that both seem impossible. A mass hallucination suddenly converting a large proportion of humanity, or Jesus coming back to life. My personal conclusion is that the evidence is at 50/50. But maybe it has been set up to be 50/50.
