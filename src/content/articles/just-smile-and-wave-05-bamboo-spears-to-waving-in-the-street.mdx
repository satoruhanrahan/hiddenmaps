# Bamboo Spears to Waving in the Street

In the final years of the Pacific War, Japanese civilian women and schoolgirls were being drilled in how to kill American soldiers with bamboo spears. This was not metaphor or propaganda gesture. It was operational preparation for Ketsu-Go, the homeland defence plan, which assumed the entire civilian population would participate in last-stand resistance against an Allied invasion. Military planners had calculated expected civilian casualties in the tens of millions and considered this an acceptable variable. The commitment was total, sanctioned from the highest levels, and taught as sacred obligation.

Japan surrendered in August 1945. Within days American soldiers were moving through Japanese streets and being met not with resistance but with curiosity, offerings of food, and in many cases open warmth. The phenomenon was widespread enough to alarm both Japanese authorities and the American occupation command. Young Japanese women pursued relationships with American soldiers in numbers nobody had predicted. The population that had been preparing to die killing the invaders was now, by most visible measures, welcoming them.

No conventional psychological framework explains the speed of this.

Relief that the war was over — yes, partially. Exhaustion — certainly. The transfer of the Emperor's authority to endorse the occupation — this carried real weight in a culture structured around imperial legitimacy. But ideology prepared to the depth of bamboo spear drills does not dissolve in days. Trauma at that scale does not simply clear. Something else was operating.

The suppression model suggests what. The wartime mobilisation had been a sanctioned release — the state had issued permission to express outward hostility, which is why the commitment was so total and so fervent. When the Emperor withdrew that permission through the surrender announcement, the suppression model reasserted itself with the same totality it always had. The licence to express was revoked. The surface snapped back to its default position.

What looked like a population switching from fanatical resistance to cheerful cooperation in days was possibly something simpler and stranger — the suppression model returning to baseline. The hostility did not disappear. The permission to show it did.

The American soldiers moving through those streets thought they were seeing a people finally at peace. They were seeing a surface. It was a perfectly maintained, entirely genuine, and completely unreadable one.

They had no way to know the difference. Neither did the Japanese people maintaining it.
