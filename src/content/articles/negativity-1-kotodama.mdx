# Kotodama: When Words Have Power

*In Japan, words are not just descriptions of reality. They participate in creating it. This ancient belief shapes everyday language in ways most Japanese people follow instinctively — and it runs directly against the kind of honest negative expression that reckoning requires.*

---

In ancient Japanese belief, words carry spiritual force. To speak something is not merely to describe it — it is to give it power, to invite it into the world, to make it more real than it was before.

This belief has a name: **kotodama** (言霊) — literally, the spirit or soul of words.

---

## How It Shapes Everyday Language

Kotodama is not a fringe superstition. It is woven into the fabric of ordinary Japanese life in ways that surface constantly.

At weddings, certain words are carefully avoided — *kiru* (切る, to cut), *wakareru* (別れる, to separate), *kowasu* (壊す, to break) — because their meanings evoke endings and severance. To say them at a celebration would be to introduce their energy into the occasion.

Hospitals avoid room numbers containing four (四, *shi*) — which shares its reading with death (死, *shi*). The number is not just unlucky. Its sound carries the thing it resembles.

New Year's (*oshōgatsu*) language is carefully, almost ritually positive. The words you use at the year's opening are understood to shape what follows. Complaints, grievances, and expressions of difficulty are deferred — not suppressed permanently, but held back from the threshold moment when words carry their greatest creative weight.

---

## The Implication for Negative Thought

The logic of kotodama extends naturally to negative mental states. If words have the power to invite what they name, then expressing negative thoughts — fear, grief, resentment, guilt — isn't just socially uncomfortable. It's cosmologically risky. You are not merely describing a negative reality. You are feeding it.

This creates a cultural orientation in which **silence about negative experience is not just polite — it is prudent**. Keeping darkness unspoken is a form of containment. Naming it risks release.

---

## The Connection to Tatemae

As explored in [Tatemae and Honne](/articles/tatemae-and-honne), Japan's public face system already creates strong pressure to manage rather than express genuine inner states. Kotodama adds a spiritual dimension to that social pressure.

Tatemae says: don't burden others with your negativity.
Kotodama says: don't give your negativity more power than it already has.

Together they form a double injunction — social and spiritual — against the open expression of negative inner life. The result is a culture where the gap between what is felt and what is said can be very wide, maintained not just by social convention but by something that runs deeper than convention.
