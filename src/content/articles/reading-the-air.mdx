In Japan there is a concept they call "reading the air" (空気を読む). It means to notice the unspoken expectations of where you are and acting within expectations without being told about them. Hearing about it for the first time, it may sound just like a cultural quirk, or it may just seem like the concept of "reading the room" in English. But it is much more than that. It is more than mere politeness. It is how Japanese society functions. It fulfils a similar role to what law fulfils in Western societies. A world where no one is "reading the air" to a Japanese person sounds like a lawless world.

## How Behaviour is Coordinated

The West does have social exclusion. Say an inappropriate joke that offends someone or fail to read the room in any way, and you may be disliked and excluded. But in Japan this dynamic is dialled up to a ten. It is deeper and heavier. This is partly because it is a shame-based culture in which morality depends on what the people around you think of you, instead of being a guilt-based culture in which morality depends on your personal conscience. And the dynamic covers things that in the West are usually covered by written rules instead. Things like workplace conduct, queueing, making too much noise.

Japan also has laws, of course, but the law almost feels secondary to the "air". At any given situation, how you are supposed to behave depends on the mood, thoughts, and actions of the people around you. The bounds of "allowed" behaviour is not written down anywhere as a common rule or law. Act within what is written in the "air" and you are considered a good person. But act outside of it — whether by talking, laughing, moving, basically anything unexpected by the people around you — and you may be seen as somewhat of an immature or lawless person. A bad person.

In any country, someone who disobeys a written rule or law is generally punished with a fine or time in prison. This is of course not what happens in Japan if you break the invisible rules written in the air. What happens is you get excluded, shunned, bullied. People will treat you coldly. In a collective society like Japan that values validation, pride, and honour, this is a big deal. It is maybe even more powerful a motivator than money, fines, or imprisonment.

## How This Makes Japan Better

Japan has a positive image and reputation abroad. It is seen as clean, efficient, polite, and this is all true. And it is all thanks to this system of reading the "air". Throwing your rubbish on the floor is not done because it violates the air. Trains run on time because being late violates the air. Customer service is exceptional in Japan because anything less would violate the air. The exceptional standards are enforced not through fines or the threat of being fired. It is the threat of social exclusion.

And the results are remarkable. From the outside, the society appears almost utopian. This reputation is what brings tourists from around the world and why the country is the focus of fascination and admiration. These results can be achieved through Japan's unique system because enforcement is efficient. There is no need for penalties such as fines. There are no inspectors, investigations or hearings required. Only the power of peer pressure.

## How This Makes Japan Worse

This system has a weakness, and it is that nothing is written down, nothing is discussed openly. So what happens when the people don't actually like what the "air" is forcing them to do? What happens when it brings more harm than good? Basically, nothing happens. Everyone keeps carrying on without even talking about it. If change ever happens, it either comes through a revolution, or it happens very slowly over generations.

Sometimes the change is too slow. The inability to adapt to a changed environment can be a problem. Why could Japan not adapt to the age of the internet and produce innovative companies like Amazon or Google? Why do they continue to overwork as if the country is still fighting for its survival? Partly because everyone is simply following the air. A junior employee or a start-up company that has a new profitable idea is more likely to be quietly rejected without being given a real chance. People tend to follow the direction set by the collective without open debate or discussion.
