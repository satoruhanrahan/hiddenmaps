# The Man on the Hill

*In the autumn of 1600, one young man's hesitation changed the course of Japanese civilisation.*

---

In the autumn of 1600, two versions of Japan went to war.

On one side, a coalition fighting to preserve the legacy of the great unifier Toyotomi Hideyoshi — a Japan governed by consensus, administered by loyal bureaucrats, power held in careful balance. On the other, forces rallying behind Tokugawa Ieyasu — a rival lord with a simpler vision: total control, with himself at the centre.

The battle that would decide it took place on a foggy morning in a mountain valley called Sekigahara. Roughly 160,000 men. A few hours of fighting. And one young man on a hillside who didn't know what to do.

---

Kobayakawa Hideaki was twenty years old and had been courted by both sides. He had pledged his support to the Toyotomi coalition. But as the armies clashed below him he sat motionless on the hillside, paralysed by doubt, unable to commit. Both sides waited on his decision. Then, under pressure he could no longer resist, he broke his pledge — and charged downhill into the flank of the very side he had promised to defend.

The battle collapsed within hours. The war was over.

---

What followed was not just a change of rulers but a transformation of Japanese civilisation itself — one whose effects reach, as this series will argue, all the way into the present.

Hideaki never recovered from what he did. He died two years later, reportedly haunted by guilt, crying out in his sleep. Some accounts say he had lost his mind entirely.

History moved on without him. But it moved the way it did because of him.
