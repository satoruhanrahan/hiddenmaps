# A Different People

*Remove the Tokugawa crucible and you get a recognisably Japanese people — but differently so.*

---

If Hideaki had kept his pledge, the Japanese people who emerged from the following centuries would have been recognisably Japanese — but differently so.

The deep aesthetic sensibility rooted in Shinto and Buddhist perception, the attunement to impermanence and season, the capacity for focused mastery — these predate Tokugawa and would have survived. But the specific psychological texture that Westerners most associate with Japanese people would look quite different.

---

Without two and a half centuries of enforced harmony, the pressure to suppress visible disagreement simply never accumulates. These alternate Japanese would likely be more comfortable with open conflict — not aggressive, but less allergically averse to stating a position plainly, less compelled to perform consensus they don't feel. Tatemae in its rigid modern form probably never develops.

Regional identity would remain strong in ways that Tokugawa unification slowly eroded. A Satsuma person would feel deeply Satsuma first. An Osaka merchant would carry a distinct civic pride. The pressure toward national homogeneity — the sense that there is one correct way to be Japanese — would be substantially weaker. More visible subcultures. More tolerated eccentricity.

---

Most significantly, the commercial psychology would be different. The Tokugawa system distrusted merchants ideologically even as it depended on them economically. In an open, trade-facing Japan shaped by powerful coastal lords invested in maritime commerce, that ambivalence never takes hold. The alternate Japanese businessman is more outward-facing, more comfortable with risk, more willing to back bold decisions over cautious consensus.

Not Western. But closer to the mercantile energy of the Koreans or the Taiwanese — civilisations that were never sealed, never frozen, never trained across generations to treat stability as the highest value.

The quiet, indirect, exquisitely self-aware Japanese character that the world recognises today is a beautiful thing. But it was not inevitable. It was made. And on a foggy morning in 1600, it almost wasn't.
