# The Machine That Never Stopped

Walk into any Japanese high school and something will strike you before a single lesson begins. The students are in uniform — navy, structured, vaguely nautical. They stand in rows at assembly. They bow to teachers. They clean their own classrooms. The whole atmosphere carries a faint military precision that feels oddly normal once you've been there long enough.

That's the point. It is normal. It has been normal for over 150 years.

In 1868, Japan did something almost without precedent in modern history. Facing the very real threat of Western colonisation — the same fate that had already swallowed much of Asia — a group of reformers dismantled their feudal government and rebuilt the country from the ground up in a matter of decades. This was the Meiji Restoration. The goal was survival, and the method was total transformation.

They built railroads, factories, a modern army, a navy. They sent students abroad to learn Western science and brought Western experts home to teach it. They created compulsory education and designed it explicitly to produce disciplined, loyal, productive subjects of the state. The 1890 Imperial Rescript on Education didn't mince words — schools existed to cultivate character in service of the nation. The nation was the point.

The military influence wasn't metaphorical. Naval curry became school lunch. The 24-hour clock became standard. Hierarchy was built into the grammar of daily life — who you bow to, how deep, who speaks first. The workplace became an extension of the barracks: the senior-junior system, the group over the individual, the expectation that you endure without complaint.

Japan modernised faster than almost any country in history. But the blueprint for that modernisation was military discipline, and it was so deeply embedded into institutions, language, and daily habit that 1945 — the surrender, the American occupation, the pacifist constitution — barely touched it.

The uniforms are still there. The hierarchy is still there. The expectation of endurance is still there.

What changed after 1945 wasn't the machine. It was the reason the machine was running. And soon, that reason would disappear too.
