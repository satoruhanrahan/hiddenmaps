# The Borrowed Purpose

After the surrender in 1945, Japan was in ruins. Major cities had been firebombed into rubble. Hiroshima and Nagasaki were ash. The economy had collapsed, food was scarce, and the country was occupied by a foreign army for the first time in its history.

And yet within a generation, Japan had become the second largest economy in the world.

This is still one of the most remarkable recoveries in modern history, and it didn't happen by accident. The same discipline, hierarchy, and collective endurance that the Meiji era had installed into schools and workplaces was redirected almost seamlessly into economic production. The barracks became the factory floor. The military campaign became the corporate campaign. The enemy was no longer a foreign army — it was economic backwardness, and the goal was to catch the West.

That goal was extraordinarily motivating because it was clear and measurable. You could watch Japan closing the gap in real time — steel output, car exports, electronics, shipbuilding. Companies like Toyota, Sony, and Honda became global symbols of what disciplined collective effort could produce. Employees worked punishing hours not because they were forced to at gunpoint but because the mission felt real. The country was going somewhere. The sacrifice had a destination.

By the 1980s Japan had essentially arrived. GDP per capita was approaching America's. Japanese companies were buying American landmarks. Analysts were seriously debating whether Japan would overtake the United States entirely. The machine built in the Meiji era, repurposed for postwar recovery, had worked beyond almost anyone's expectations.

Then in 1991 the asset bubble collapsed. But the timing was worse than it looked, because something else was happening simultaneously. The global economy was shifting toward software and technology — industries that reward exactly what the Meiji machine had spent a century suppressing. Individual creativity, lateral thinking, flat hierarchies, the willingness to challenge authority, comfort with ambiguity and failure. Japan, one of the most technologically sophisticated countries on earth, produced almost nothing of global significance in software. No Japanese Google, no Japanese Amazon, no Japanese Apple. The culture and the new economy were a near-perfect mismatch.

The Lost Decade became two, then three. Lifetime employment guarantees quietly disappeared. Salaries stagnated. The implicit promise that endurance and loyalty would be rewarded stopped being kept.

The machine kept running. The hierarchy was still there, the long hours were still expected, the bowing and the seniority system and the pressure to conform were still fully intact.

But the destination was gone.

A society built to endure anything — as long as it was enduring toward something — was suddenly enduring toward nothing. And that created a psychological problem that Japan has never quite solved.
