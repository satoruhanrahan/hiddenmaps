# The Void and the Villain

When a society loses its purpose, it doesn't announce it. There's no moment of collective realisation, no public reckoning. Instead, the symptoms appear quietly, in the behaviour of ordinary people trying to navigate a system that no longer makes sense.

In Japan, the symptoms were hard to miss if you knew what you were looking at.

From the mid-1990s onward, young men began withdrawing entirely from society. Not leaving the country, not protesting, not rebelling in any visible way. Just retreating to their bedrooms and not coming out. Sometimes for months. Sometimes for years. The Japanese gave it a name: hikikomori. By some estimates, over a million people were living this way by the 2000s.

Around the same time, young Japanese men were losing interest in the things their fathers had organised their lives around — career advancement, marriage, status. Birth rates were already falling and kept falling. Young people were choosing quiet non-participation over a system that had stopped rewarding participation.

These weren't random trends. They were a generation doing a rational calculation. The deal that had made the machine bearable — work hard, endure, and prosperity will follow — had been broken. Unlike their parents, who had paid too high a price to admit the deal was bad, younger Japanese hadn't bought in enough to need to defend it. So they simply stepped aside.

But birth rates tell only part of the story. South Korea — which shares Japan's extreme work hours, its rigid corporate hierarchy, and its cultural pressure to endure without complaint — has the lowest birth rate of any country on earth. Falling birth rates affect most developed countries. But cultures that make it structurally impossible to question the system seem to fall faster and further than others.

Something else was happening simultaneously among the generation that had bought in. For them, admitting the pointlessness wasn't psychologically possible. A different solution was needed, and it arrived in familiar historical form: the external villain.

From the 1990s onward, nationalist sentiment grew steadily louder. China's rise was framed as a threat. Korea's demands for wartime accountability were framed as persecution. The narrative that Japan was being treated unfairly by a hostile outside world became increasingly mainstream. Politicians built careers on it.

The void had found its villain. The machine had found a new reason to run.
