# The American Trap

There's a question that sits underneath all of this that rarely gets asked directly: why didn't Japan go through the same psychological reconstruction as Germany after the war?

Germany was de-Nazified in a serious, sustained way. War crimes were prosecuted, the ideology was dismantled, and an entirely new national identity was built around guilt, responsibility, and European integration. It was painful and imperfect, but the operating system was genuinely replaced. Modern Germany is almost defined by its reckoning with its own history.

Japan got something different. The Tokyo War Crimes Tribunal prosecuted some military leaders, but the Emperor — the symbolic centre of the entire imperial project — was preserved and protected by the Americans. The zaibatsu industrial conglomerates were broken up and then quietly reassembled. Left-wing labour unions that might have challenged the old order were suppressed. Militarists who had been purged in the early occupation were rehabilitated and returned to public life.

This wasn't an accident or an oversight. It was a strategic decision that hardened as the Cold War took shape. China fell to the Communists in 1949. The Korean War started in 1950. Japan was suddenly the most important piece on the American strategic board in Asia — a forward base, a bulwark against Soviet and Chinese influence, an indispensable ally. A destabilised, psychologically uprooted Japan was no use to anyone. A functional, disciplined, anti-communist Japan was exactly what was needed.

So the Meiji machine was preserved, deliberately, because it was useful.

Japanese nationalists are aware of this history, and they have a genuine grievance about it — though not quite the one they think they have. Their complaint is that Japan was psychologically manipulated into weakness, taught a masochistic version of its own history through American-influenced textbooks and the pacifist constitution. They want to restore Japanese pride, reframe the war as defensive, and rebuild national confidence.

The irony is almost elegant. The Americans preserved Japan's discipline and mobilisation culture because it was strategically useful. Now Japanese nationalists want to restore the ideological confidence to go with it — a remilitarising Japan with a reasserted national identity — which is also, as it happens, exactly what Washington wants as a counterweight to China today.

They are trying to escape a trap by walking deeper into it. The manipulation they're resisting and the outcome they're pursuing both serve the same master.

The cage was built by others. But the nationalists are now insisting, loudly, that it was built by Japan.
