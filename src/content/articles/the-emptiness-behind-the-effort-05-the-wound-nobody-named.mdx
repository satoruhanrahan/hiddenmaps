# The Wound Nobody Named

Before the West arrived, East Asia was not a region on the brink of catastrophic militarism. China was an ancient, stable civilisation that had largely chosen to limit its own imperial ambitions. Japan was a feudal society that had been deliberately, consciously closed to the outside world for over two centuries. The region had its conflicts and its dynastic struggles, as every region does. But the scale of violence, the arms races, the paranoid nationalism, the authoritarian mobilisation — none of that was inevitable. It was produced.

In 1839 Britain went to war with China because China had the audacity to destroy British opium shipments that were addicting its population by the millions. Britain won. China was forced to legalise the opium trade, hand over Hong Kong, and open its ports to foreign commerce on foreign terms. When China resisted further demands fourteen years later, Britain and France burned the Emperor's Summer Palace to the ground. Western powers then spent the rest of the century carving China into spheres of influence — each claiming ports, territories, and trade rights from a civilisation they were systematically dismantling.

Japan was watching all of this happen to its neighbour. Then in 1853 American warships sailed into Edo Bay and delivered what was essentially an ultimatum — open your ports and your markets, or face the consequences. Japan looked at China and understood exactly what the consequences meant.

The Meiji Restoration wasn't an expression of Japanese imperial ambition. It was a survival response. The militarism, the discipline, the authoritarian mobilisation, the drive to build a modern army and navy and industrial base — Japan copied the Western imperial model because that model was the one that was winning. It industrialised not because it wanted an empire but because having an empire was the only proven protection against becoming one.

The West has spent decades reckoning with its colonial history in Africa, South Asia, and in its own treatment of indigenous peoples. That reckoning has been incomplete and often performative, but it has been real enough to change Western self-image in fundamental ways. What has never been part of that conversation — not seriously, not publicly — is the direct causal line from Western colonial aggression in East Asia to Japanese militarism to the Pacific War to the unresolved tensions burning through the region today.

Japan is asked to apologise for the middle of a story whose beginning has never been honestly told.

That doesn't change what Japan did. The atrocities were real and the victims deserve acknowledgment. But the moral accounting everyone demands of Japan is sitting on top of a much larger moral accounting that has never been submitted. The wound that set everything in motion has never been named by the people who made it.

Until it is, the reckoning will remain incomplete — for everyone.
