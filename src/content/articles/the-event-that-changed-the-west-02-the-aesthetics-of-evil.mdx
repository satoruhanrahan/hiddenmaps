# The Aesthetics of Evil

*When an idea becomes associated with a specific image, the image becomes more dangerous than the idea itself.*

By the time the Nuremberg trials concluded, something had happened beyond the legal reckoning. A particular visual and rhetorical vocabulary had been permanently contaminated. The rallies, the uniforms, the salutes, the rhetoric of blood and soil and national destiny — these were no longer just the style of a defeated regime. They had become the recognisable aesthetic of industrialised murder. Anyone reaching for that vocabulary, even partially, was not just making a political argument. They were activating a pattern recognition in their audience that led directly to the camps.

This created a problem for nationalism that has never fully been solved. Nationalism as a political force did not disappear after 1945. The desire for cultural continuity, border control, civilisational identity, and the prioritising of one's own people over outsiders — these impulses are old and persistent, and no amount of moral architecture eliminates them from human political life. What changed was the aesthetic through which they could be expressed. The old vocabulary was poisoned. New containers had to be found.

The rebranding happened gradually and not always consciously. European nationalist movements across the second half of the twentieth century discovered through repeated failure that the explicit vocabulary of racial nationalism collapsed their support the moment it surfaced. The association with Hitler was too immediate, the pattern recognition too fast. A movement could hold almost any position if it found the right language. The same position in the wrong language was finished before it started.

Marine Le Pen understood this more clearly than almost anyone. When she inherited the Front National from her father — whose version of French nationalism was explicit enough about its antisemitism to trigger exactly that pattern recognition — she embarked on what she called dédiabolisation. The rebranding was meticulous and deliberate. The party was eventually renamed. The rhetoric was softened, lawyered, redirected toward culture and sovereignty rather than race and blood. The substance shifted less than the packaging, but the packaging was everything, because the packaging was what determined whether voters could allow themselves to vote for it without seeing themselves in a certain mirror.

Giorgia Meloni performed a version of the same operation. Her party's roots in post-fascist Italian politics are well documented, and the flame symbol in her party's logo is a direct inheritance from that tradition. But Meloni governs in suits, speaks the language of conservative democracy, and is careful about which historical comparisons she invites. The aesthetic distance from 1930s fascism is maintained with precision, because that distance is the political product being sold as much as any policy.

What the Holocaust produced, in other words, was not the elimination of nationalist politics. It produced the forced sophistication of nationalist politics. Movements that might otherwise have remained crude were compelled by the weight of the taboo to develop more refined containers for their ideas — containers that could pass through the checkpoint of the Hitler association without triggering it.

For several decades, the wall largely held. Nationalist movements existed at the fringes of Western politics through the second half of the twentieth century but remained electorally weak and culturally marginal. The taboo was doing its structural work. The water was contained.

But then something happened that the wall's architects hadn't planned for. Immigration into Western Europe accelerated to a scale that created real, visible cultural friction — in neighbourhoods, in schools, in public debates about values that previously seemed settled. People could see it and feel it in their daily lives, but the political language for discussing it honestly had been taken off the table. You could not talk about it without someone reaching for the Hitler comparison. So the conversation didn't happen — not openly, not honestly. The pressure built without release. And pressure without release doesn't disappear. It finds another way out. The nationalist movements now reshaping Western politics are the result — and they needed a new kind of leader to carry them. One that nobody could easily compare to Hitler.
