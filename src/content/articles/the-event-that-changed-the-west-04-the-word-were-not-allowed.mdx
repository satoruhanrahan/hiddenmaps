# The Word We're Not Allowed

*There is a difference between a word that is dangerous and a word that has been made to seem dangerous. Confusing the two has consequences.*

Almost everyone in the Western world now agrees that racism is wrong. That agreement is one of the genuine moral achievements of the postwar era — hard won, institutionally embedded, and worth defending. The problem is what got bundled in with it. Somewhere in the decades between Nuremberg and now, the taboo on racial thinking quietly expanded until it swallowed something it was never meant to cover. It swallowed culture.

Race and culture are not the same thing. Race is biology — immutable, inherited, morally irrelevant to how a person should be treated. Culture is something else entirely. It is the set of values, behaviours, social norms, and ways of organising life that a group develops over time. Culture can be criticised. It can be compared. It can be found wanting in specific respects without any implication that the people who hold it are lesser human beings. This distinction seems obvious when stated plainly. In contemporary Western political discourse it is almost impossible to maintain.

Say that certain cultural attitudes toward women are incompatible with equality and you will be heard, by a significant portion of your audience, as saying something about race. Say that some immigrant communities are integrating poorly and you will be accused of racism before you have finished the sentence. Say that national cultures are different in ways that matter and produce different outcomes and you will find yourself explaining, defensively, that you are not Hitler. The collapse of the distinction between race and culture is so complete that honest conversation about one has become almost impossible without triggering the defences built around the other.

This did not happen by accident. It happened because race and culture were genuinely fused in the ideologies that produced the Holocaust and the broader history of Western colonialism. When European empires justified themselves by calling other peoples primitive or savage, they were making racial and cultural claims simultaneously, and the racism was doing the work. Unpicking that fusion after the war meant treating cultural criticism with suspicion, because cultural criticism had so often been racial hierarchy in disguise. The caution was understandable. The problem is that it calcified into a reflex that stopped making distinctions.

The cost of that blind spot is not abstract. Immigration debates cannot be had honestly if cultural compatibility is off the table. Integration policy cannot be designed honestly. Questions about which values a society insists on as non-negotiable cannot be asked honestly. The difficult conversations don't disappear because the language for having them has been made radioactive. They go underground, where they fester and simplify and eventually explode in the cruder forms that serious political discourse was supposed to prevent.

The great irony is that the taboo, built to protect liberal democratic values, has made those values harder to defend. A society that cannot openly discuss whether certain cultural practices are compatible with gender equality, freedom of speech, or the rule of law is not a society confidently defending those things. It is a society hoping nobody notices the contradiction. People notice. And when the only politicians willing to name what they are seeing are the ones the taboo was designed to exclude, the taboo has defeated itself.

Recognising this is not a concession to racism. It is the opposite. The distinction between race and culture is one of the most important tools liberal democracy has for navigating a diverse world honestly. Surrendering that distinction to avoid difficult conversations doesn't protect anyone. It just ensures the conversations happen badly, loudly, and too late.
