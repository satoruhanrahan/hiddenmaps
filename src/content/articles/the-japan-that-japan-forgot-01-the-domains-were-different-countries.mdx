# The Domains Were Different Countries

Most people think of Japan as one culture with regional variations. Like how Osaka people are louder, or Kyoto people are more refined. Interesting flavour differences, but fundamentally the same country underneath.

That's not what Edo Japan was.

The han — the domains controlled by each lord — weren't administrative regions of a unified nation. They were closer to separate countries that happened to share a writing system and a distant emperor nobody paid much attention to. A farmer born in Satsuma in the south and a merchant's son born in Edo would have had almost nothing in common except the language, and even that was touch and go given how thick the regional dialects ran.

This matters because when people talk about traditional Japanese culture — the group harmony, the deference, the emotional restraint — they're usually describing one specific regional type that got elevated into a national identity later. But we'll get to that. First you need to feel how different the domains actually were.

Take Satsuma, down in Kagoshima. The culture there was militaristic in a way that made the rest of Japan look soft. Satsuma men were famously silent, physically hard, and deeply suspicious of outsiders — including other Japanese. Their dialect was so impenetrable that historians have seriously suggested they used it deliberately, as a kind of encryption. These were not people interested in managing your feelings or maintaining group harmony. They were interested in being formidable.

Now put them next to Tosa, over on the Pacific coast of Shikoku. Tosa produced a lower samurai class called gōshi who had unusual independence and a reputation for being argumentative, direct, heavy drinkers, constitutionally unimpressed by rank. Sakamoto Ryōma — the man who helped broker the alliance that ended the shogunate — was pure Tosa. Anti-hierarchical, entrepreneurial, willing to work with anyone regardless of domain if the goal was right. A Satsuma man would have found him exhausting. A Tosa man would have found a Satsuma man suffocating.

Then there's Kaga, on the Japan Sea coast. The second wealthiest domain in the country, but specifically forbidden from building military capacity by the Tokugawa. So they put everything into art instead. Lacquerwork, ceramics, Noh theater, textile dyeing. Two centuries of redirected energy produced a culture so refined it still feels distinct today.

Three domains. Three completely different human types. Different values, different emotional styles, different ideas about what a good man looked like.

Japan wasn't one culture. It was a collection of them, held together by political force and a shared mythology neither side fully believed.

What happened to all that difference is a story worth telling.
