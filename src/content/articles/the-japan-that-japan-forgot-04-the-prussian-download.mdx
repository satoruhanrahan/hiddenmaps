# The Prussian Download

In the 1870s, the Meiji government sent a delegation of Japanese officials on an eighteen-month tour of the Western world. They visited America, Britain, France, Germany. They were trying to solve a specific problem: how does a small island nation modernize fast enough to avoid being colonized by the powers currently carving up the rest of Asia?

They came back with a lot of answers. For the military, the answer was Prussia.

This wasn't a casual decision. Bismarck's Germany had recently unified a collection of fractious independent states into a single powerful nation through disciplined military force and top-down institutional reform. That was almost exactly the problem Japan was trying to solve. The Meiji oligarchs looked at Prussia and saw a mirror of their own ambitions. They imported the general staff system, the conscript army structure, the military education model, and something harder to name but more important — the idea that the individual soldier's will should be completely dissolved into the unit.

Here's what's worth understanding about that last part. It wasn't natural to Japan at the time. The Satsuma warriors who formed the backbone of the early Meiji military were proud, semi-independent fighters who had personal loyalty to their lord and a strong sense of individual martial identity. The new conscript army asked them to become anonymous and interchangeable. Many of them hated it. The Satsuma Rebellion of 1877 was partly a revolt by men who found the new military culture alien and degrading.

The Prussian model won anyway because it worked at scale. And then something important happened.

The men who went through that military system came out the other side and went into civilian life. They took jobs, started companies, ran ministries. They brought the template with them. The absolute deference to superiors regardless of competence. Seniority over merit in promotion. Collective responsibility for individual failure. The idea that being visibly present and enduring signals loyalty more than results do.

The early corporations were structured by men who had been militarily socialised. The great postwar companies inherited those structures and refined them into the salaryman system. The office became the barracks in civilian clothing.

Then the Meiji state did something even more consequential. It passed the Civil Code of 1898, which created the ie — the household system. Every family was legally structured around a male head with near-absolute authority. This was presented as ancient Japanese tradition. It was a legal invention six years old.

Then came the Imperial Rescript on Education, drilling loyalty and collective subordination into every child in the country through a standardised national curriculum.

Within two generations, a population that had included Tosa hotheads and Edo ironists and Satsuma stoics had been largely processed into a single behavioral type.

And that type got called traditional Japanese culture.

Nobody thought to ask when the tradition had started.
