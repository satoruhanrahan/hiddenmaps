# The Tradition That Was Built Yesterday

There's a particular kind of person in Japan who spends their weekends in military-era uniforms, riding sound trucks through city streets broadcasting speeches about Japan's sacred traditions, or gathering at Yasukuni shrine with a passion that makes ordinary citizens quietly step aside. The uyoku dantai — the Japanese nationalist right. Loud, theatrical, utterly convinced they are the last guardians of authentic Japan against the forces of foreign contamination.

The irony is so complete it almost hurts to look at directly.

Start with Yasukuni itself. It feels ancient. The shrine architecture, the solemnity, the weight of the dead. But Yasukuni was founded in 1869. It's younger than the American transcontinental railroad. It was created specifically by the Meiji state to enshrine soldiers as gods who died in service to the emperor — a theological construction designed to motivate conscripts to die willingly. The idea that fallen soldiers become divine through imperial service has no deep roots in Shinto. It was engineered by a modernising government that needed a religious technology to feed a modern army.

The emperor theology they defend with equal ferocity has the same problem. The emperor as the living sacred centre of Japanese civilisation, the divine axis around which all national identity orbits — this is a Meiji invention assembled from fragments of ancient ritual and a great deal of 19th century German Romantic nationalism. The actual historical emperor had been a ceremonial figurehead sidelined by regents and shoguns for the better part of a thousand years. Ordinary Edo people related to the emperor the way most people relate to a distant symbolic institution — with vague reverence and almost no personal feeling.

Then there's Bushido — the samurai code the nationalists treat as Japan's ancient spiritual backbone. Loyalty, honor, death before surrender, the warrior soul of the Japanese people. It feels like it must be centuries old.

The version they know was written in English in 1900 by a Christian diplomat named Nitobe Inazō, living in America, recovering from illness, writing explicitly for Western readers. He wanted to explain Japan to people raised on European chivalry, so he used European chivalry as his template. Theodore Roosevelt loved it and bought copies for his friends.

Then it got translated back into Japanese. And the Japanese reading public received it as a rediscovery of their own ancient soul — not realising they were reading a document composed in English for foreigners by a man who had converted to Quaker Christianity. The feedback loop closed completely. The Western-facing construction became internal self-understanding.

The actual samurai texts tell a different story. The Hagakure is strange, obsessive, and dark. Other samurai writings are frankly bureaucratic — household accounting, administrative procedure, the practical anxieties of a retainer class that hadn't fought anyone in generations. The Edo-period samurai was mostly a minor official pushing paper. None of that made it into Nitobe's version because it didn't serve the narrative the Meiji state needed.

The shrine is 1869. The emperor theology is German Romantic nationalism. The warrior philosophy is a 1900 English-language publication.
