# The System That Needs You Weak

Every system of control faces the same problem. If the people it controls become capable, confident, and self-directing, the system has no reason to exist. So the system — not always consciously, not always cruelly — tends to produce the opposite.

This is not a conspiracy. It is a structural tendency. Systems built around managing human weakness have a quiet incentive to maintain it.

## The loop in action

Colonial education offers the clearest example. British administrators in India built schools, but designed them to produce clerks rather than thinkers — people fluent enough in English to serve the empire, not educated enough to challenge it. When those same administrators pointed to low rates of Indian self-governance as evidence that Indians were "not yet ready" for independence, they were citing a condition they had engineered. The incapacity was real. Its cause was invisible to them.

The same loop appears in foreign aid. Decades of dependency-creating assistance have left some recipient nations less able to sustain themselves than before the aid arrived. Institutions built to deliver food, medicine, and infrastructure from outside displaced the local systems that once did the same work imperfectly but indigenously. When aid organisations then observe that these nations "cannot function without support," they are often observing the residue of their own interventions.

## Closer to home

The loop is not unique to large political systems. It runs through families. A parent who steps in to resolve every difficulty their child encounters will, over time, produce a child with a low tolerance for difficulty. The parent experiences this as evidence that the child needs protecting. The child experiences it as the normal texture of life. Neither can easily see the mechanism at work.

## Why it never stops

What makes the loop so durable is that it is self-confirming at every stage. The controller intervenes because people seem incapable. The intervention prevents people from developing capability. People remain incapable. The controller intervenes again. At no point does the evidence suggest the controller should stop, because the evidence is being produced by the controlling.

This is what separates a self-justifying system from ordinary mistake-making. An ordinary mistake can be corrected when evidence accumulates against it. A self-justifying system generates its own confirming evidence. The proof of necessity is the damage the system causes.

## The true believer

The controllers in these systems are rarely cynical. Most genuinely believe they are helping. That belief is part of what keeps the loop closed. A cynical actor might eventually be troubled by the gap between stated purpose and actual effect. A true believer experiences no such gap. What they see confirms what they already think.

The question the loop never gets to ask is simple: what would these people look like if we had never intervened at all? That baseline is always unavailable. It cannot be retrieved. And so the system never has to face the possibility that the weakness it exists to manage is, in large part, a weakness it made.
