# When Evil Believes It Is Good

Most people who cause serious harm don't think of themselves as harmful. That's not a cynical observation. It's just true. The people who ran the systems we now look back on with horror — colonial administrators, institutional wardens, ideologues of every stripe — mostly went home at night feeling like they were doing something necessary. Something good, even.

That's the part that's hard to sit with. It would be easier if they'd known. If somewhere in the back of their minds there was a voice they were choosing to ignore. But often there wasn't. The belief was genuine. And that genuine belief is exactly what made the whole thing so hard to stop.

## When the belief is genuine

When someone does harm on purpose, they can eventually be confronted with what they've done. The evidence lands differently when you weren't trying to hide from it. But when someone has spent years — or a lifetime — organising their whole sense of themselves around the idea that they are one of the good ones, the evidence doesn't just challenge what they did. It challenges who they are.

Think about what it would actually mean for a British colonial administrator in 1900 to fully accept that the system he'd devoted his career to was not civilising India but hollowing it out. It wouldn't just mean admitting a policy mistake. It would mean that the thing he'd told himself every morning — that he was there to help, that the work mattered, that the hardship was worth it — was a story he'd been telling himself over a reality he'd never looked at directly.

## The slow reckoning

Most people can't do that in one go. So they don't. What tends to happen instead looks something like this: first, they find reasons the evidence is wrong. Then they start noticing things that didn't bother them before. Then they get defensive when the subject comes up. Then, if they're honest, something quietly breaks. Not dramatically. Just a slow loss of certainty that can't quite be put back.

The ones who never get there — who go to their graves certain they were right — aren't necessarily more dishonest than the ones who do. They're often just more committed. The belief had more of their identity wrapped up in it. Letting go would cost more.

## The same at every scale

What makes this more than just a historical observation is that the same thing happens at every scale. The parent who controlled their child out of love. The manager who micromanaged out of responsibility. The friend who couldn't stop giving advice nobody asked for. The harm in these cases is smaller, but the psychology is identical. Good intentions, a genuine belief in the necessity of their involvement, and an almost complete inability to see what their involvement was actually doing.

The uncomfortable truth is that "I meant well" and "I caused harm" are not opposites. They sit together all the time. Probably more often than we'd like to think. The question isn't whether someone's intentions were good. It's whether they were ever willing to look past their intentions at what was actually happening.

That willingness — to look, really look, even when it costs you the story you've been telling about yourself — might be the hardest thing a person can do. Most systems, and most people, find a way to avoid it entirely.
