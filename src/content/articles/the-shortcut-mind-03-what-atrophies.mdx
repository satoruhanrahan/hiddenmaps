# What Atrophies

When cars became common, people walked less. Their legs still worked. They just stopped needing them as much, and over generations the average person became less capable of covering long distances on foot. The same thing is probably happening to certain thinking skills right now, and it is worth knowing which ones.

The most obvious is memory. Smartphones have already changed this. People used to memorise phone numbers, directions, facts they needed regularly. Now they do not bother, because why would you. The ability is still there but the habit is gone. What gets lost is not just the facts themselves but the web of connections between them, which is often where actual understanding lives.

Concentration is going the same way. Sitting with one difficult problem for an hour without checking anything, without relief, without a shortcut — this was already getting harder before AI. The default response to confusion is now to reach for a device. AI makes that escape even faster and more satisfying, because you get not just a distraction but an answer.

Then there is the ability to construct an argument from scratch. If you can always ask AI to make the case for something, you practise building your own reasoning less. You might still be able to evaluate an argument when you see one. But generating one — starting from nothing and working through to a conclusion — is a different skill, and it weakens without use.

The most important one, though, is tolerating uncertainty. This is the capacity to sit with a question you cannot answer yet, stay curious rather than anxious, and resist grabbing the first available explanation. It sounds simple but it is genuinely difficult and it genuinely requires practice. And it is the foundation of everything else on this list. You cannot reason carefully if you cannot tolerate not knowing. You cannot audit an AI's answer if you need the uncertainty to end immediately.

This is what makes the atrophy of uncertainty tolerance different from forgetting phone numbers. Forgetting phone numbers is inconvenient. Losing the ability to sit with hard questions means losing the ability to think independently at the moment when independent thinking matters most — which is exactly when the AI might be wrong and you need to catch it.

The skills that are most at risk are not the flashy ones. They are the slow, uncomfortable ones that nobody enjoys practising. Which is precisely why they are at risk.
