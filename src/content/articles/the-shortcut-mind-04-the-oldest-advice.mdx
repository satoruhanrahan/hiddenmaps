# The Oldest Advice

If AI can reason, synthesise, and calculate better than you, there is not much point competing on those terms. The more useful question is where you actually have an advantage — and it turns out the answer points directly at the things that make a good life in the first place.

There are things AI cannot do at all. It cannot want anything from the inside, shaped by a specific life and specific losses. It cannot feel what it is like to be somewhere, lose someone, or finally understand something that has confused you for years. It cannot have a real relationship where both people are vulnerable and the stakes are genuine. And it cannot generate the questions that come from actually living — the ones that keep returning because they matter to you specifically. These are not gaps that better AI will eventually close. They are the territory that remains entirely yours. And they are also, not coincidentally, what most people would say a meaningful life is made of.

Then there are two skills that sit alongside these. Endurance of uncertainty — the ability to hold an unresolved question without immediately reaching for an answer — and discernment of AI accuracy — the ability to evaluate whether what the AI produced is actually right. Neither can be outsourced. A model cannot audit itself with genuine scepticism, and it cannot sit with not knowing. Those jobs stay human. And both require the same slow, uncomfortable inner work that the rest of this series has been arguing against abandoning.

The practical strategy that follows is straightforward even if it is not easy. Stop spending energy competing where AI is strongest and start investing in the things it cannot touch. Slow down enough to know what you actually want rather than what you have been nudged into wanting. Build relationships that have real stakes. Stay present in experiences rather than just consuming information about them. Follow the questions that your specific life generates. And maintain enough independent reasoning to catch AI when it is wrong, which it will be, often smoothly and convincingly.

This is not new advice. Humans have been giving versions of it to each other for thousands of years. What has changed is that the case for following it is now stronger than ever — not just because it leads to a better life, but because it turns out to be the only strategy that makes sense.
