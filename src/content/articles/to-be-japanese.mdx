There is a small moment that happens repeatedly when Japanese people introduce themselves to foreigners. Before profession, before city, sometimes even before name — they say they are Japanese. And they say it with a particular weight, as though it explains something fundamental about them.

This isn't unique to Japan in the sense that people everywhere have national pride. But the quality of the identification is different. When an Australian says "I'm Australian" it is descriptive — a fact about where they are from. When a Japanese person says "I am Japanese" it carries something closer to an ontological claim. It is a statement about what they are at the deepest level, not merely where they happen to have been born.

The distinction matters. Western national identity is largely civic — a political arrangement that people belong to. You can be fully Australian while being Vietnamese, Lebanese, or Greek. Nationality sits on top of other identities without absorbing them. It is one layer among several.

Japanese identity doesn't work this way. Being Japanese is ethnic, cultural, linguistic, and national all fused into a single largely inseparable package. The boundary between self and nation is unusually thin. Japan isn't somewhere Japanese people are from — it is, in a meaningful sense, what they are.

This shows up beyond the introduction moment. Japanese people abroad often experience criticism of Japan as personal criticism. They feel a pull to represent and defend their culture even when they privately disagree with what they're defending. The group travels with them.

None of this is a character flaw. The fusion produces genuinely remarkable things — extraordinary cultural continuity, collective pride in craft and tradition, a sense of shared responsibility for how Japan appears to the world. But it has consequences worth examining, particularly when Japan confronts its own history.

Before examining those consequences, the more fundamental question is worth sitting with: why is Japanese identity constructed this way? Why does the self-nation fusion run so deep when it doesn't in most Western cultures?

The answer involves geography, religion, agriculture, deliberate political engineering, and two centuries of enforced isolation — all pointing in the same direction simultaneously.
