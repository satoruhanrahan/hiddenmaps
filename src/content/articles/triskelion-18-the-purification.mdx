# The Purification

Izanagi emerges from Yomi into the living world and goes immediately to water.

He is defiled — contaminated by contact with death, with decomposition, with the realm of the dead. In Shinto thought this defilement has a specific name: kegare. It is not moral failure or sin in the way Western religious traditions understand those concepts. It is a kind of ritual pollution — a disruption of the vital, generative quality of life caused by proximity to its negation. Death is not evil. But contact with it leaves a residue that must be cleansed before the living can fully participate in the world again.

Izanagi goes to a river — in some versions the sea — and performs misogi, the purification ritual of washing. He removes his clothing and jewelry, each discarded item becoming a kami as it touches the water. He washes his body. The defilement of Yomi dissolves in the running water.

And from this washing — from the act of cleansing rather than from any deliberate creative intention — the three most important kami in the entire Japanese tradition are born.

From his left eye: Amaterasu — the Great Deity Who Illuminates Heaven. The sun goddess. The supreme sacred figure of the Yamato tradition and the divine ancestor of the imperial line.

From his right eye: Tsukuyomi — the Moon Counter. The moon deity, ruler of the night realm.

From his nose: Susanoo — the Impetuous Male. The storm deity, the most dramatically human of the three, the figure who will generate the most mythology of anyone in the text.

These three — the Mihashira no Uzu no Miko, the Three Precious Children — are assigned dominion over the three realms. Amaterasu governs Takamagahara, the Plain of High Heaven. Tsukuyomi governs the realm of night. Susanoo governs the sea.

The structural significance of their birth is extraordinary. The highest sacred figures in Japanese mythology are not born from deliberate creation. They are born from washing off defilement. The sun herself emerges from the act of cleansing rather than the act of making.

This encodes something the Shinto tradition understood deeply — that purity is not the absence of contact with darkness. It is what emerges when you wash the darkness off and return to the water.

The most sacred things come from the most necessary cleansing.
