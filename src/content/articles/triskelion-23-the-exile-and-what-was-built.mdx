# The Exile and What Was Built

The heavenly assembly's verdict on Susanoo is unanimous. He is fined heavily and expelled from heaven permanently.

Susanoo descends — not to the sea, the realm he was assigned and refused, but to Izumo. The Japan Sea facing the Korean peninsula. The region that will become the most significant sacred landscape outside the Yamato sphere.

He arrives at the upper reaches of the Hii River and finds an old man and woman weeping over their daughter. Seven daughters have been taken by Yamata no Orochi — the eight-headed serpent that comes every year demanding tribute. The eighth, Kushinadahime, is about to be taken. Susanoo offers to defeat the Orochi in exchange for her hand.

His method is not direct combat. He uses sake — a fermented rice product, a specifically agricultural and continental technology — to neutralize the serpent before striking. The Orochi drinks from eight vats, falls into a stupor, and Susanoo cuts it apart. Inside the serpent's tail he finds a magnificent sword — later named Kusanagi, the Grass-Cutter — which he will offer to Amaterasu.

What the Orochi was is genuinely uncertain. The standard readings — a flood, a rival clan, a local strongman controlling the iron sand river — are all possible and not mutually exclusive. One reading developed in this series, offered as interpretation rather than established fact, suggests the Orochi might encode a specific chieftain controlling the Hii River's iron sand resources, with Susanoo's continental knowledge providing the decisive advantage in the coalition that defeated him. This cannot be verified. The Orochi might simply be a dragon.

What is certain is that after the defeat, Susanoo stays.

He marries Kushinadahime. He finds a place at Suga and composes what is considered the first waka poem in the Japanese literary tradition — eight clouds rising over the mountains of Izumo, the eightfold fence built to shelter his bride. Not about celestial authority or military victory but about clouds over a specific landscape and the domestic act of building shelter for another person.

One possible reading of this behavior is that it encodes the integration process of arriving continental people into Jomon communities — the marriage into the local family, the planting of trees, the poem honoring the specific place as the mythological memory of genuine integration rather than conquest. This reading cannot be verified. But if the myth is remembering something, the pattern of what it shows — the outsider arriving, defeating the oppressive force, marrying in, settling down, writing a poem about the clouds — looks less like conquest and more like the beginning of a community.

His descendants will eventually produce Okuninushi. Whatever the synthesis was that Susanoo began, it produced the most beloved deity in Japan.

It started with eight clouds over a mountain an exile decided was worth writing about.
