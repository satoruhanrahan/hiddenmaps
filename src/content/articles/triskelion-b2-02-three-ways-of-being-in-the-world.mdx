# Three Ways of Being in the World

Every landscape contains a mountain, a river, and a sea.

Not always literally — but always structurally. Every human community has a relationship with high ground, with flowing water, and with open water or open plain. These three geographic relationships produce three fundamentally different ways of organising human life — and the same three-part structure appears to recur at every scale, from a single valley to the entire planet.

The Mountain People live in relationship with high ground, forest, and the specific sacred landscape of a particular place. Their economy is based on hunting, gathering, herding, or small-scale farming in complex terrain. Their community is small enough that everyone knows everyone. Their sacred tradition tends toward animism — the mountain, the river, the ancient tree inhabited by presences that must be respected and negotiated with.

The River People live in relationship with flat, fertile, irrigated agricultural land. Their economy is based on wet rice or grain cultivation at scale. Their community is large enough that you regularly interact with people you don't personally know. Their sacred tradition tends toward hierarchy — the sky-god whose authority descends from above legitimating the human hierarchy that manages the water and the surplus.

The Sea People live in relationship with open water. Their economy is based on trade, fishing, and the movement of goods between communities. Their community is defined not by a specific landscape but by a network of connections across many landscapes. Their sacred tradition tends toward cosmopolitanism — the divine encountered in the journey, in the meeting of different worlds, in the harbour where all traditions are temporarily equal.

These are not racial categories, ethnic categories, or fixed national identities. They are civilisational orientations — tendencies produced by specific environmental conditions and economic logics. The same person can embody all three depending on context. The same nation contains all three in different proportions.

What differs is which orientation dominates — which pole's values, sacred objects, governance structures, and trust systems most thoroughly shape the overall culture.

One clarification matters here, because it is easy to overstate. Geography does not produce a pole directly. What geography actually shapes is group size, mobility, and how often a community deals with strangers it will meet only once. Mountain terrain tends to cap group size and keep membership stable, which makes personal verification both possible and necessary. Agricultural surplus enables large, settled, stratified populations too big to verify personally but stable enough to sustain durable offices. Coastlines and trade routes force contact with strangers no personal or institutional tie can reach, which only an impersonal mechanism can answer.

Stated this way, the claim becomes falsifiable rather than a story that can explain any outcome after the fact. It predicts that any group — regardless of terrain — will develop Mountain-style personal trust below a certain stable size, will improvise Sea-style mechanisms wherever stranger-contact is high and no institution reaches far enough, and will fragment if it scales past personal verification without building either role-structure or mechanism. Desert Arabia is the clearest historical test: real Mountain trust at the tribal level, exactly as the terrain would suggest, and separately a caravan-trade stranger-contact problem solved not by geography's obvious answer but by a Sea-style mechanism built on a shared religious oath instead of state contract law. Geography set the starting conditions; it did not write the outcome by itself.

The framework's central claim is that the health of any civilisation depends not on one pole winning but on all three remaining in productive tension. The same claim made visually by the mitsudomoe. Three forces rotating around a centre. None of them stopping. None of them winning.
