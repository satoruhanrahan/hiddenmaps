# How Trust Works

Trust is the foundation of every social system. The three poles produce three completely different answers to the question of how you get people to cooperate — and these answers are partially incompatible in ways that generate much of the political and cultural conflict in any civilisation that contains all three.

**Mountain People trust: personal and direct.**

You trust someone because you have watched them over time in many situations. Reputation travels through the community via storytelling. Violation is catastrophic and inescapable. The system is robust within the community and very difficult to extend beyond it. The outsider is untrustworthy by default — not as ideology but as the rational limit of a system that requires personal knowledge to function.

**River People trust: institutional.**

You trust the position, not the person. The judge, the magistrate, the emperor — trustworthy because they occupy positions within a sanctioned hierarchy. This scales to millions. But it depends entirely on the institution enforcing its own rules. Corruption at the top destroys the entire system because the system's legitimacy rests on divine sanction — and a corrupt hierarchy has visibly lost its mandate.

**Sea People trust: impersonal mechanisms.**

Contract, currency, reputation networks, rule of law. These make cooperation possible between complete strangers who share neither personal history nor institutional framework. But they only extend to those who can participate. And they can fail systemically — everywhere at once — in ways that personal and institutional trust do not.

**Three depths within each pole:**

Within all three poles, trust can be built at different depths, and the depth matters as much as the pole itself. Earned trust means you personally verified this exact person, office, or mechanism yourself, over time. Mediated trust means you haven't verified it yourself, but someone you already trust has, and vouches for it directly — a specific, traceable chain rather than a vague impression. Proxy trust means no verification chain exists at all — only a surface signal standing in for one: a resemblance, a uniform, a certification badge. The same structure recurs in all three poles, and each pole's proxy tier carries its own characteristic exploit — false kinship for Mountain, impersonation for River, forged certification for Sea.

**Charisma: borrowing trust it hasn't earned:**

Charisma is not a fourth way of trusting someone. It is a performance that triggers proxy trust on whichever pole the audience actually runs on — manufactured intimacy for a Mountain audience, manufactured competence for a River audience, manufactured promise of mutual benefit for a Sea audience. What makes it dangerous is exactly what makes it effective: it produces the feeling of earned trust, compressed into a single encounter, without any of the history that is supposed to earn it. A charismatic founder, a demagogue, and a confidence man are running the identical manoeuvre on three different poles.

**Why only three:**

Two further candidates for a fourth kind of trust are worth naming, because both were tested seriously before being folded back into the existing three. Trusting a stranger purely because they share your ethnicity, dialect, or faith looks distinct at first, but turns out to be Mountain trust running on a proxy signal rather than earned history — the same mechanism as charisma, just sincere rather than performed. And trusting someone because they swore an oath before God looks distinct as well, but resolves as Sea trust with a different enforcement backend: the same structure as an ordinary contract — impersonal, indifferent to who the person is, binding only on those who recognise the enforcing authority — just running on divine sanction instead of a court. Neither candidate survived as a genuine fourth. That two serious attempts to break the trichotomy both reduced to the existing three is real evidence, not proof, that trust has exactly three possible attachments: the verified person, the verified role, and the verified mechanism.

**The tensions between them:**

Mountain People distrust River People institutions because institutions can become extractive — the official who is trustworthy by position turns out to be the worst threat to the community. They are right. Institutions fail in exactly this way.

Mountain People distrust Sea People mechanisms because contracts and money treat everything as potentially exchangeable — including things that should not be exchanged. They are right. Commercial logic commodifies what it touches.

River People distrust Sea People individual freedom because individual optimisation disrupts collective coordination. The merchant who maximises personal profit can externalise costs onto the community that sustains him. They are right.

Sea People distrust River People institutional authority because hierarchies capture commercial systems — taxing trade, applying relational rather than equal justice. They are right. Hierarchy exploits the commercial networks that generate the wealth it extracts.

**The synthesis problem:**

What the three-pole civilisation is trying to build is a system where all three trust mechanisms work simultaneously — personal trust in the small community, institutional trust in the larger framework, impersonal trust in the commercial network — without the incompatibilities between them becoming catastrophic.

This is extraordinarily difficult. It has never been fully achieved. But the civilisations that come closest — that maintain all three trust systems in productive rather than destructive tension — appear to produce measurably better outcomes for the people who live in them.

The prescription is not complicated to state. It is very difficult to maintain.

All three. Simultaneously. In tension.
