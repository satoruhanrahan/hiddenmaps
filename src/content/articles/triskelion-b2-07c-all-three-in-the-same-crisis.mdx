# All Three in the Same Crisis

No modern event demonstrated the three poles' different crisis responses as precisely as COVID-19 — because it was a genuinely global crisis that every civilization had to respond to simultaneously, making the differences between responses directly comparable.

The River People's response was lockdowns, contact tracing, centralized coordination, and mass vaccination campaigns. Effective at the scale it could reach. In its maximum expression — China's zero-COVID policy — it worked until it couldn't adapt. The ideology that justified the consolidation prevented accurate assessment of the point at which the strategy needed to change. The hierarchy suppressed the information that the strategy was failing until the failure was impossible to suppress.

The Sea People's response was vaccine development through distributed innovation and commercial incentive, rapid supply chain adaptation, and financial absorption of enormous economic shock. The mRNA vaccine platform — developed at commercial speed under emergency authorization — was a genuine Sea People innovation triumph. The shadow side was equally visible: vaccine nationalism, profit extraction, and the systematic widening of inequality between the networked and the unnetworked. The Sea People fixed the crisis for those inside the network and exported the remaining costs to everyone else.

The Mountain People's response was community mutual aid networks, local food production, the intensification of community bonds, and the return to what was reliable and close. Almost entirely invisible from the perspective of those expecting decisive action from above. Not photogenic. Not politically legible as a crisis response. But extraordinarily effective at the human scale where it operated — the neighbors feeding each other, the local networks activating, the community knowing who needed help and providing it without institutional coordination.

No single response was adequate on its own.

The River People's coordination couldn't develop vaccines or adapt when the strategy needed to change. The Sea People's innovation couldn't reach the communities the trading network didn't serve. The Mountain People's mutual aid couldn't operate at the scale the crisis required.

The civilizations that handled COVID best had enough of all three functioning simultaneously — institutional coordination at scale, adaptive commercial innovation, and community mutual aid in the spaces institutions and markets couldn't reach — without any single pole crowding out the others.

Which is the mitsudomoe's permanent argument.

Three forces. Each doing what it does best. Each constrained by the others from doing what it does worst.

The center holds when all three are turning.
