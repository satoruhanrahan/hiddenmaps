# Counterfeit and Defection

Every one of the three trust systems can fail in two completely different ways, and the framework so far has only accounted for one of them.

The first way is counterfeit: the signal was fake from the start. A stranger performs intimacy he hasn't earned. A conman wears a uniform he has no right to. A certificate claims an audit that never happened. Counterfeit failure is exactly what proxy trust describes, and it has a clean remedy — deeper verification catches it, because there was never anything real behind the signal to begin with.

The second way is defection, and it is a genuinely different animal. Here the trust was real. It was earned honestly, verified repeatedly, and it held — right up until the moment it was profitable to betray it. A fund that pays real, verified returns for twenty years before collapsing. A colleague with a real track record, loyal for a decade, until activated. A trading platform that honours every transaction for years and then vanishes with the balances on its way out. In every one of these cases, more verification would not have helped — if anything, it would have made the eventual betrayal more profitable, by giving the defector a longer, more convincing history to cash in.

This means every cell in the trust system — Mountain, River, and Sea, at any depth — carries two distinct failure modes rather than one: a counterfeit failure that better verification defeats, and a defection failure that better verification cannot defeat and may even reward.

A related case sharpens this further: the sincere false prophet. A leader who genuinely believes a failed prophecy is not running a counterfeit — there is no performance, nothing insincere about the belief. And there is no defection either — there is no planned exit, no harvest moment, the leader goes down with everyone else. The trust is catastrophically misplaced without anyone actually lying. Sincerity and accuracy turn out to be independent of one another, and destructive misplaced trust doesn't require deception at all.

One finding here matters more than it first appears. Mountain's earned tier — real, personally verified history between two specific people — turns out to be the most defection-resistant cell of the entire system. The very feature that stops Mountain trust from scaling, that it requires real and costly personal history to build, is exactly what makes betraying it costly and rare. Cheap trust is cheap to betray. Expensive trust is expensive to betray. This gives the Mountain pole a second, independent justification for its place in the triad, beyond the original Borromean argument: it is not just the check on River's inversion and Sea's abstraction, it may be civilisation's only real defence against the patient defector who plays every other system honestly until the moment it pays not to.
