# The Mitsudomoe

In the Nara period — the same decades that produced the Kojiki — a symbol began appearing on the roof tiles of Japanese shrines and temples.

Three comma-shapes rotating around a center. Each comma the shape of a magatama jewel, a water drop, a curling wave. Three of them arranged so that each one's head nestles against the next one's tail, the whole system turning perpetually around a point that belongs to all three and to none of them individually.

The mitsudomoe.

Its appearance alongside the Kojiki in the same cultural moment is suggestive — both the founding synthesis text and the visual symbol encoding the synthesis appearing together in the Nara period. Whether this was deliberate coordination or parallel expression of the same cultural project is impossible to say. But the convergence is striking.

The mitsudomoe's strong association with Hachiman is revealing. Hachiman's cult originated at Usa Shrine in northern Kyushu — the Sea People's primary node, with deep connections to the Korean peninsula and the continental arrivals. In 749 CE his cult was formally brought to Nara to protect the great Tōdai-ji temple — the most ambitious Buddhist construction project in Japanese history, itself a continental import arriving via the Sea People's Korea route. A deity from northern Kyushu, carrying the Mountain People's magatama symbol, incorporated into the River People's imperial capital.

Whether this movement was consciously understood as a three-pole synthesis or simply as a politically useful religious incorporation is unknown. But the structure it produced — a deity combining all three traditions, symbolized by the rotating-three image — fits the pattern we have been tracing throughout this series.

The Celtic triskelion arrived at the same symbol independently. Three spiraling legs rotating around a center. Found at Newgrange before 3200 BCE. Becoming the symbol of the Isle of Man and Sicily — both islands where multiple civilizational currents converged without any single one achieving complete dominance.

Two civilizations. Opposite ends of the Eurasian landmass. No contact between them. The same rotating-three symbol.

The mitsudomoe is not a declaration of achieved synthesis. It is a permanent question encoded as a symbol: are all three still rotating? Is any one of them dominating? Is the center still holding?

Every time the symbol appears — on a shrine roof tile, on a family crest, on a taiko drum — it is asking that question. Not declaring an answer.

The answer is always the work of the present moment.
