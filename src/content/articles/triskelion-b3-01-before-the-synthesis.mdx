# Before the Synthesis

Japan was not always one civilization.

For approximately fourteen thousand years before the continental arrivals, the Japanese archipelago was home to the Jomon people — one of the longest-continuous cultures in the archaeological record. They were not a single uniform tradition but a diverse collection of communities shaped by the specific landscapes they inhabited: the inland forest hunters of Tohoku, the coastal maritime fishers of the Japan Sea, the river valley communities of the central plains.

Reading the three-pole structure back onto the Jomon is interpretive — but the ecological logic is suggestive. The inland communities lived in the specific, rooted relationship with sacred landscape that we associate with the Mountain People. The coastal communities of the Japan Sea were sophisticated maritime operators — fishing tuna in open water, producing shell ornaments that traveled through exchange networks across the archipelago. The river valley communities were beginning to cultivate plants in ways that anticipated the agricultural transition.

The Jomon were not pre-civilizational. Their dogu figurines — clay figures with elaborate decoration, predominantly female in character — suggest a sophisticated sacred tradition. Their Itoigawa jade distribution network moved sacred objects from the Japanese Alps across the entire archipelago, implying organized long-distance exchange. Their coastal settlements show evidence of complex social organization and ritual practice.

Then, beginning around 300 BCE, the Yayoi people arrived — primarily through the northern Kyushu corridor, from the Korean peninsula. They brought wet rice agriculture, bronze and iron metallurgy, continental administrative knowledge, and a population that would, over several centuries, demographically transform the archipelago.

The genetic evidence is now reasonably clear on what happened. The Jomon population was not replaced — it was diluted. Yayoi ancestry is densest in the agricultural heartland of western Honshu and northern Kyushu. Jomon ancestry is highest in the geographic peripheries — Okinawa, Hokkaido, the Tohoku region, and certain Japan Sea coastal areas. The pattern of genetic distribution traces the pattern of agricultural expansion — the River People's civilization moving eastward along the Inland Sea corridor, with the Mountain People's traditions surviving most completely in the margins the expansion found least profitable.

The three poles were not imported together as a package. They arrived sequentially, met unevenly, and negotiated their relationship across centuries before anyone tried to formalize that negotiation in a text.

The Kojiki is the record of that formalization. But the encounter it was trying to synthesize had been unfolding for a thousand years before the text was written.
