# The Yamato Synthesis

By the 7th century CE the Yamato state had achieved sufficient political dominance over the Japanese archipelago to undertake something ambitious — the compilation of a founding mythology that would legitimate its authority while incorporating the traditions it had encountered and absorbed.

The result, completed in 712 CE, was the Kojiki.

The reading applied throughout this series — that the Kojiki encodes three distinct civilizational traditions corresponding to what we have called the Mountain, Sea, and River People — is our interpretive framework, not an established scholarly position. What follows applies that framework to the Kojiki's political structure. The evidence is structural rather than documentary — we are reading a pattern into the text rather than reporting what historians recognize the text as doing. With that caveat clearly stated, the pattern is suggestive enough to develop carefully.

The text begins with a synthetic first principle — Amenominakanushi — that no single clan could claim, placed above the competing supreme kami of rival traditions. It gives the Izumo tradition a permanent cosmological home — the hidden world, governing the invisible forces of fate and relationship — rather than simply suppressing it. It preserves the northern Kyushu maritime tradition in the sacred mirror that became the most important imperial sacred object.

Whether the compilers understood what they were doing in these terms is impossible to know. What is clear is that the Kojiki's political settlement with Izumo — the pacification of the land mythology, Ōkuninushi yielding the visible world in exchange for governance of the hidden world — was a genuine negotiation rather than a simple conquest. The Izumo tradition retained its shrine, its hereditary priesthood, its sacred geography, and its cosmological function. It was not erased. It was incorporated on terms that preserved its essential character.

This is the difference between the Yamato synthesis and most imperial mythological projects. Most conquering traditions simply replace the traditions they conquer. The Kojiki's compilers — for whatever combination of political pragmatism, genuine philosophical sophistication, and cultural memory — chose a more complex approach.

They built a text that could say to all three traditions simultaneously: you are real, you are honored, you belong here. And none of you is the whole story.

Whether Japan has lived up to that promise is the question its subsequent history keeps answering differently.
