# The Twentieth Century

The twentieth century was the most catastrophic expression of River People dominance in recorded history — and the overcorrection that followed has created problems of its own.

The specific failure mode in the century's first half was not simply that the River People's pole dominated. It was that the River People's pole captured the emotional energy of the other two poles while eliminating their capacity to provide counterweight.

Nationalism captured the Mountain People's sacred landscape attachment — the specific language, the specific territory, the specific ancestral tradition — and weaponized it in service of the River People's hierarchical state. The folk tradition's love of the homeland became the industrial state's justification for territorial expansion. The Mountain People's roots now had tanks.

Industrial capitalism captured the Sea People's commercial energy and innovation while eliminating Sea People cosmopolitan tolerance and impersonal rule of law. The factory system was not the harbor — it was the paddy field with machinery. Workers as inputs to an industrial process rather than as individual traders. The Sea People's productive dynamism serving the River People's hierarchical organization of labor.

The result — in Germany, Japan, and the Soviet Union — was River People dominance more totalizing than anything the pre-modern world had produced. The industrial military machine gave the hierarchy unprecedented mobilizing capacity. The nationalized Mountain People tradition gave it unprecedented emotional legitimacy. What it could not do was adapt. When the hierarchy's strategy was wrong, no mechanism existed to correct it. The River People's crisis response — consolidate, demand conformity, suppress contrary information — became the operating mode of entire civilizations.

1945 was the result in Germany and Japan. 1991 was the result in the Soviet Union.

The postwar correction swung decisively toward the Sea pole — the liberal international order, the Washington Consensus, the financialization of everything, the belief that markets could solve problems markets had created. And this correction has generated its own crisis.

Extreme inequality as the natural result of unrestrained individual competition. The commodification of healthcare, education, and community life. Social atomization — the community dissolved into a collection of autonomous individuals. Short-term financial thinking replacing the long time horizon. The 2008 financial crisis as the Sea People's trust system failing systemically.

The River People's resurgence — China's explicit rejection of Sea People universalism, Russia's civilizational assertion, nationalist movements across the Western democracies — is partly a genuine reaction to Sea People overreach. Not justified in all its expressions, but structurally intelligible. When the Sea People's commercial logic dissolves everything — community, tradition, national identity, sacred landscape — the River People's counter-response is predictable.

The 21st century's central civilizational question is whether this oscillation — River pole dominance producing catastrophe, Sea pole overcorrection producing its own crisis, River pole resurgence threatening another catastrophe — can be interrupted by a genuine three-pole rebalancing. Or whether the pendulum will keep swinging between the two dominant poles while the Mountain People's tradition — the ecological limits, the community bonds, the sacred landscape — continues to be destroyed by both.

The mitsudomoe's question is the same in 2025 as it was in 712 CE.

Are all three still rotating?
