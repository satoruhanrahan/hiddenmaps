# The Three Poles and Crisis

Crisis is when the dominant pole intensifies and the suppressed poles become either the resource that saves you or the absence that makes things worse.

The Mountain Person withdraws. Turns toward the people and places they know and trust. Reduces exposure. This is genuinely adaptive when the crisis is a loss requiring grieving and stabilizing — the Mountain Person's ability to stay present to painful reality without needing to immediately fix or escape it is one of the most valuable responses available. The shadow is that the withdrawal which stabilizes in the short term can become permanent retreat. The waiting-out strategy fails when the crisis is structural rather than temporary — when the world has changed in a way that will not reverse.

What the Mountain Person needs from the other poles in crisis: the River Person's structural discipline to keep basic functions going when forward motion is difficult, the Sea People's capacity to find the new route when the old one is genuinely gone.

The River Person consolidates. Applies more structure. Makes lists. Finds the procedure that will solve the problem and executes it. This is genuinely adaptive when the crisis requires organized sustained effort — building back after loss, managing a complex problem requiring sequential steps, holding the structure while others are overwhelmed. The shadow is that consolidation can become compulsive control — structuring everything as a defense against anxiety, using productivity as a way of not feeling what has happened.

What the River Person needs in crisis: the Mountain Person's willingness to actually feel the loss rather than immediately organizing a response, the Sea Person's adaptability when the established procedures stop working.

The Sea Person moves. Finds a new angle, a new connection, a new approach. Pivots to what's possible rather than grieving what's lost. This is genuinely adaptive when rapid adaptation is required — when the old route is genuinely closed and a new one needs to be found. The shadow is that movement can prevent the necessary experience of loss. Pivoting away from the painful reality before it has been fully processed.

What the Sea Person needs in crisis: the Mountain Person's willingness to stay with what has happened and let it be what it actually was, the River Person's structural discipline when adaptability generates too many options and not enough commitment to any.

The person who can draw on all three states in crisis — present to the painful reality, maintaining basic structure, finding the creative adaptation — is the most resilient.

Not because they are three times as capable. Because the Borromean structure applies at the individual scale exactly as it applies at the civilizational one. Remove any single state and the other two become pathological without the counterweight.
