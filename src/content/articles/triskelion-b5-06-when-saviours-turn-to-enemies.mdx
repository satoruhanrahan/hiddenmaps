# When Saviours Turn to Enemies

A sacred object gives people something to belong to, something to work for, a sense that their own conduct matters to an outcome larger than themselves. It is one of the most powerful forces in human civilisation. It is also, depending on one detail, either mostly harmless or the most dangerous idea a society can hold.

The detail is whether restoration requires an enemy.

## The Two Versions

A society can be devoted to something — proud of shared institutions, working toward a common future, feeling that what we build now will matter long after we are gone — without any of that depending on another group's diminishment. Call this the building version of a sacred object.

But there is another version, where the sacred thing's story is structured around suffering — something was done to us, by someone, and restoration means correcting that wrong at the wronging party's expense. Call this the grievance version.

The two can look similar from a distance. Both involve devotion to something larger than the self. Both involve a story about past and future. Only one of them has a historical track record of producing mass violence.

## The Mechanism

The twentieth century's catastrophes — traced in an earlier article to the River People's pole dominating and capturing the other two — can also be read through this lens. The grievance sacred object is specifically what went wrong. Nations that felt humiliated rather than merely proud. Restoration narratives that pointed at a specific enemy rather than a common project.

It is tempting to conclude that the fix is to return to religion rather than nationalism, since religion is less geographically bounded. But the evidence doesn't support this. Religious revival under pressure doesn't reliably produce the building version. Sometimes it produces exactly the same grievance structure — God's people wronged, an enemy of God to be confronted. The Crusades ran on this engine. The mechanism is identical regardless of which sacred object it operates through.

## The Question Worth Asking

The variable that actually matters is not which object — nation, God, tradition, cause — but whether the object's story is structured around grievance. A sacred object oriented toward building tends to stay safe regardless of what it is. A sacred object oriented toward correcting a wrong done by someone else tends to become dangerous regardless of what it is.

Under pressure, people reach for sacred objects almost as a reflex. Pressure doesn't pull toward the careful, building version. It pulls toward whichever version is fastest and most emotionally satisfying — and the grievance version is almost always faster.

The question worth asking of any sacred object people are reaching for right now is not what it is. It's which version is on offer.
