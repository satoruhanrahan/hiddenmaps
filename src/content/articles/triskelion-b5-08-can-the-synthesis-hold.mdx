# Can the Synthesis Hold

The synthesis is never achieved. It is only maintained — imperfectly, provisionally, through continuous effort against the tendency of any single pole to capture the others.

The Kojiki's political settlement gave the three traditions their cosmological addresses and sent the balance forward into history. Whether its compilers understood what they were doing in the terms this series uses is impossible to know. They were attempting political synthesis — honoring multiple competing sacred traditions within a single framework, negotiating the terms under which the Izumo tradition would accept Yamato authority. Our reading of this as a three-pole structure is ours, applied retrospectively. The pattern we find in the text may be more deliberately constructed than we can verify, or it may be a structure that emerged from the political negotiation without anyone fully naming it.

The mitsudomoe is a different case. The rotating-three symbol, consciously deployed on shrine roof tiles in the same cultural moment the Kojiki was compiled, suggests someone understood that three forces in dynamic balance was the right image for what the synthesis was trying to hold. Whether they understood those forces in anything like the terms we are using — Mountain, River, Sea; animistic, hierarchical, commercial — is unknown. The symbol encodes the structure. What it names in its creator's mind is beyond recovery.

What we can say is that the structure has proven remarkably durable. The Meiji period dismantled it. The postwar settlement partially restored it. The corporate hierarchy erodes it daily. The kami in the shrine forests maintain it daily. The synthesis holds imperfectly and the imperfection is the normal condition, not the exception.

At the civilizational scale the elimination of geographic protection has removed the shelter that once allowed the Mountain People's tradition to survive in the margins the River and Sea People's civilizations found unprofitable. Whether institutional design can replace what geography once provided remains urgent and unanswered.

At the individual scale the question is whether you can maintain three-pole balance in a life that the civilization around you is continuously pushing toward one or two poles at the expense of the third. The answer is: with effort, yes. Not perfectly. But yes.

The symbol on the shrine roof tile is a practice instruction.

Three forces rotating around a center. The center doesn't hold the forces still — it holds them in relation to each other. The system is alive in the rotation, not in any single element's dominance. Stop the rotation and you don't get stability. You get death.

The synthesis is not a state to achieve. It is a movement to maintain. The work of noticing which pole has been neglected and returning to it. The work of noticing which pole is dominating and finding the others as correction.

The work is still ongoing. At every scale from a single life to the entire planet.

The mitsudomoe still turning.

The question still open.

The synthesis still worth attempting.
