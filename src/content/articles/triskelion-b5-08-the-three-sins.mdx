# The Three Sins

Every pole has a characteristic way of betraying its own best instinct. Not through obvious selfishness — the failure is subtler than that. It happens when the strength curls inward and starts doing the opposite of what it was for.

## The Mountain Sin: Exclusion

Mountain People protect what is sacred. The land, the ancestors, the community — these things matter and deserve protection. That instinct is real and good.

The shadow appears when protection becomes the point. When the sacred stops being something you give yourself to and starts being something you use to decide who is in and who is out. The temple that was built for worship becomes a boundary. Purity becomes a weapon. The question shifts from how do we honour what is holy to who is holy enough to be here.

The people who get excluded aren't abstractions. They're the ones standing outside.

## The River Sin: Inversion

River People build institutions that serve the community. The hierarchy exists for a reason — to coordinate, to protect, to carry out something larger than any individual. At its best, it works.

The shadow appears when the institution starts serving itself. The hierarchy that was supposed to channel power downward begins extracting it upward. Loyalty to the structure replaces loyalty to the purpose the structure was built for. The bureaucrat protects the system. The priest protects the church. The official protects the office.

The people who needed the institution to work are the ones who pay for it when it doesn't.

## The Sea Sin: Abstraction

Sea People pursue universal truth and justice — principles that apply to everyone, regardless of who they are or where they come from. This is genuinely powerful. It's also the hardest failure mode to spot.

The shadow appears when the principle becomes a reason not to act. You understand structural poverty too well to give money to the man outside the station. You know the system is broken but the calculation says your individual action won't change it. The framework does the moral work of saying no, and it does it with a clean conscience.

The person in front of you is still there. The abstraction just made them easier to step around.

Three poles. Three sins. Same disease underneath — the strength that folds back on itself and stops serving the people it was meant for.
