Hierarchy exists in every society. But it doesn't feel the same everywhere — and the difference isn't just a matter of degree. It's a matter of structure.

In Japan, hierarchy activates in the encounter. Walk into a restaurant and you are a customer; the person serving you is a server. Those roles carry real weight — in language, in behaviour, in expectation. Step out of the restaurant and the hierarchy dissolves. Meet that same person at a party and the dynamic shifts entirely. The hierarchy described a relationship in a context, not a permanent ordering of two human beings.

In Victorian England, hierarchy didn't work like that. Your place in the order was determined by what you *were* — your family name, your wealth, your education. A servant wasn't below you in this interaction. They were below you. The hierarchy described the person, not the encounter.

## Relational vs ontological

The distinction is between relational hierarchy and ontological hierarchy. Relational hierarchy organises people according to their roles in a given situation. Ontological hierarchy organises people according to their fundamental nature — what they are, not what they're doing.

The geography of hierarchy article explains where each came from. Fragmented land in Japan produced intense competition between domains and clans. Hierarchy organised around those competing units — around role, rank, and function within a group. Fragmented land in Europe produced the same competition, but the primary competitive unit became the family bloodline itself. Over generations, that logic hardened into something more absolute: some families were simply better than others, some people simply ranked lower by birth.

## When hierarchy went global

The consequences of this difference became most visible when Western hierarchy was exported through colonialism. The ontological logic travelled with it — the idea that certain people were inherently, permanently ranked below others, not by role or context but by nature. Race became the ultimate ontological category: a hierarchy that followed you everywhere, activated in every encounter, and could never be dissolved by leaving the room.

This is categorically different from the Eastern relational model. However demanding Japanese hierarchy is — and it is genuinely demanding — it has never claimed to describe what a person fundamentally is. It describes where they stand in this moment, in this relationship.

## The wrinkle

Japan had its own fixed hierarchies. The imperial bloodline was absolute. The burakumin — a hereditary outcast class — were subject to discrimination that looked more Western than relational. The East isn't purely relational, and the West isn't purely ontological.

But the dominant, felt experience of everyday hierarchy differs. One system tells you your place in this room. The other tells you your place in the world. One you can leave when the encounter ends. The other follows you out the door.
