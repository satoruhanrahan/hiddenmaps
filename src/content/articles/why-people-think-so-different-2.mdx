*Japan and the West may be very different now, but rewind several hundred years and they look a bit more similar.*

---

Both had warrior classes — the knights and samurai — who passed their roles from father to son, ruling over the peasant population. The peasants meanwhile worked on land they did not own, passing their positions down the generations.

But move over to China and the story is a little different. The peasants there were more likely to own the land they worked on. Instead of knights and samurai, they had scholars, administrators, and wealthy gentry. Through the Confucian examination system, a peasant of exceptional ability could rise into the imperial bureaucracy. Sometimes ability was more important than who your parents were.

## The Two Landscapes Shaping Two Cultures

Why was this the case? Look at the geography. The Japanese and Europeans lived around fractured seas and fragmented land, of mountains and islands. Powerful families would compete with each other for control over small pockets of land, and access to lucrative sea trading routes. This created more pressure for innovation but also for hereditary hierarchy.

The large, flat floodplain of northern China on the other hand, created more pressure for large scale cooperation. They needed to utilise all the fertile land as well as survive the floods. This created pressure for a stable, collectivist society of people who needed to prioritise coordination over local territorial competition. And this culture was imported over the ocean into Japan, which developed a hybrid culture of fractured sea and floodplain cultural elements.

## Confucian Hierarchy in East Asia

The Confucian system emphasises roles and relationships. Which is why in modern Japan, if you enter a restaurant, a relationship of roles is formed. A customer is above the server in that moment. Your language, behaviour, and expectations depend on who is above and who is below in the relationship. But leave the restaurant and meet the same server at a party, and the hierarchy is gone, and you are equals.

## Hereditary Hierarchy in the West

Hierarchy tended to work differently historically in Western societies. It was more based on who you were. Your education, family, reputation, wealth. The hierarchy often did not dissolve depending on where you met, and it was more permanent. This is why with expansion and colonialism, the hierarchy could more easily shift into racism. But this system also created the pressure for people to resist corrupt authority, to demand democracy, and to create an impressive legal system.

The influence of different landscapes and how we survived in those landscapes created the cultures we were raised in. All of this affects how you think, how you see yourself, and how you see society. So next time you meet someone of another culture who thinks differently to you, consider that maybe the way they think, or the way you think, was fostered by thousands of years of survival in a certain type of geography.
