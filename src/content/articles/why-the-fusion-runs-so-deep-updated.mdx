Most national identities are relatively loose. They sit on top of other identities — family, religion, ethnicity, profession — without absorbing them. For national identity in Japan to become as total as it did, something unusual had to happen. In fact several things had to happen simultaneously, all pushing in the same direction.

Start with geography. Island separation meant Japan developed in genuine isolation for long stretches — no land border bringing foreign peoples, competing languages, or alternative identities into daily contact. This created the conditions for an unusually coherent and self-contained culture — one with a strong tendency toward total group identification.

Shinto deepened this spiritually. The islands themselves are divine in Shinto cosmology — born from the gods Izanagi and Izanami. The Emperor descends from Amaterasu, the sun goddess. Belonging carries a quasi-sacred dimension that precedes rational choice. Cultural membership and cosmic order become the same thing.

Rice farming reinforced it practically. Wet rice cultivation requires intense coordinated labour — irrigation, planting, harvesting — in ways other farming doesn't. Centuries of genuine interdependence created a deep cultural premium on collective cohesion over individual interest, not as ideology but as survival.

These forces produced a culture with an unusually strong tendency toward total group identification — a tendency that expressed itself first at the domain level. A farmer in Satsuma identified with Satsuma completely. Loyalty ran to the local lord and the han with the same totality that would later attach to the nation.

Meiji simply scaled this upward. Faced with Western imperial pressure, Japan's new leaders codified national identity through schools, the military, and state Shinto — building kokutai, the family-state, with the Emperor as father and the people as his children in one sacred organic unity. They didn't invent the tendency. They aimed it at a single national entity.

This is why it worked so quickly. They weren't asking people to adopt an alien concept. They were redirecting a deeply familiar one.

The result was a national identity that feels, to those who carry it, simply like what they are. What happened next was almost inevitable: that feeling became a theory.
