At some point in the postwar decades, Japan's sense of its own distinctiveness stopped being a feeling and became a literature.

From the 1960s through the 1980s, a genre emerged that would come to be called *Nihonjinron* — theories of Japaneseness. The books sold in enormous numbers. They made claims that went well beyond cultural observation: that Japanese people processed information differently, perceived nature more holistically, felt emotional states that had no equivalent in other languages. Japan wasn't merely a culture with its own customs and history. It was a different order of human experience entirely.

What distinguished Nihonjinron from ordinary national pride was its biological dimension. The uniqueness it described wasn't acquired — it was inherited. Japanese sensitivity, Japanese group cohesion, Japanese aesthetic attunement were expressions of Japanese blood, refined through millennia of shared island life. Culture and ethnicity were not merely intertwined. They were the same thing. To be Japanese was to carry something in the body that could not be learned, taught, or transferred.

This has a specific consequence. When culture is understood as an expression of biology, it can no longer be evaluated as a set of contingent human choices — choices that could have been made differently, choices that can be criticized or rejected. It becomes something closer to a physical characteristic. And criticism of a physical characteristic is not critique. It is prejudice.

The pattern this produces is consistent. Suggest that tatemae creates a kind of institutionalized dishonesty, or that Japanese work culture causes measurable harm to the people inside it, and the criticism arrives not as an observation about changeable conditions but as evidence of how the speaker feels about Japanese people. The substance is bypassed entirely. What is heard is hostility. Nihonjinron doesn't produce a sensitivity to criticism. It produces a framework in which criticism and discrimination become genuinely difficult to distinguish.

It is when this framework meets Japan's wartime history that its consequences become most serious. The demand for accountability from China or Korea is received not on its historical merits but as evidence of what those countries feel about Japan — resentment, rivalry, the settling of old scores. An attack on Japan is experienced as an attack on me. The filter is already in place before the argument begins, and once an argument has been categorized as tribal hostility, it no longer requires an answer. It only requires a defense.
